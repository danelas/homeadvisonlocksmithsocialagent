High-Security Keys: Why They Can't Be Copied at the Store

Published June 19, 2026

What Makes a Key High-Security or Restricted?

A high-security key uses a patented keyway design that manufacturers control tightly. Unlike standard Kwikset or Schlage keys, which use widely available blanks, restricted keys require blanks sold only to authorized locksmiths and dealers who verify your credentials before cutting duplicates.

The physical key may also include advanced features: sidebars, telescoping pins, angled cuts, or magnetic elements that defeat standard lock-picking and bumping. But the restricted blank is what stops casual copying. No kiosk at the grocery store stocks these blanks, and no hardware clerk can order them without factory authorization.

Common restricted keyway brands include Medeco, Mul-T-Lock, Abloy, ASSA, Schlage Primus, and EVVA. Each uses a unique patent and distribution model, but the principle is the same: you can't walk into a retail shop and duplicate the key without proof you own the lock.

Why Retail Stores and Kiosks Can't Copy Restricted Keys

Retail key-cutting machines are stocked with standard blanks — the most popular profiles used in millions of locks. When you hand a clerk a high-security key, the machine simply won't have a matching blank. Even if they wanted to help, the keyway is proprietary and not for sale through wholesale channels.

Beyond inventory, authorization matters. Distributors of restricted blanks require locksmiths to register with the manufacturer, verify business credentials, and sometimes track every blank sold. This audit trail prevents unauthorized duplicates from entering circulation.

Some kiosks use optical scanners and claim to copy "any key," but they still depend on available blanks. A patented Medeco or Mul-T-Lock profile won't match anything in the machine's carousel. You'll get an error or a polite refusal.

How Restricted Keyway Systems Protect Your Property

When a former employee, tenant, or contractor has a key, you need control over who can make copies. Standard keys can be duplicated at dozens of retail locations without your knowledge. Restricted keys close that loophole.

To get a duplicate high-security key, the holder must visit an authorized locksmith and often present a key card or authorization form you issued when the lock was installed. The locksmith verifies the request, checks the card against their records, and only then cuts the key. You maintain a log of who has keys and when duplicates were made.

This control is critical for commercial properties, apartment buildings, and any facility where master keying or sub-master keying is used. If an unauthorized duplicate enters the master system, an entire building's security can be compromised. Restricted keyways prevent that scenario.

High-security locks also meet stricter standards. Many are rated ANSI/BHMA Grade 1, meaning they withstand higher force, more cycles, and advanced attack methods than Grade 2 or 3 locks. Pair a restricted keyway with a Grade 1 deadbolt, and you've significantly raised the bar for intruders.

Common High-Security Key Brands and Systems

Medeco uses rotating sidebar pins and a patented keyway. Keys are marked with a card number, and duplicates require authorization. Widely used in government and commercial settings.

Mul-T-Lock employs telescoping pins and angled cuts. Their Interactive+ line adds movable elements inside the key itself. Very difficult to pick or bump.

Abloy relies on rotating disc technology rather than pin tumblers. The keyway is unique, and blanks are tightly controlled in North America and Europe.

Schlage Primus combines a standard pin-tumbler mechanism with a sidebar. You get compatibility with existing Schlage hardware while adding restricted keyway protection.

ASSA and EVVA offer European-style high-security cylinders with magnetic coding or complex sidebars. Less common in the U.S., but available through specialized locksmith services.

When to Upgrade to a Restricted Keyway System

Consider high-security keys if you manage a commercial building, rental property, or any facility with high turnover — employees, contractors, cleaning staff. Each time someone leaves, you can't be certain they didn't copy a standard key before handing it back.

Rekeying repeatedly gets expensive. A restricted keyway lets you issue keys confidently, knowing duplicates require your explicit permission. When someone leaves, you collect the key and cross them off your authorization list. No surprise duplicates down the line.

Homeowners also choose restricted keys for peace of mind. If you've had contractors, housekeepers, or previous owners with keys, upgrading to a high-security lock with a restricted blank ensures no old copies work. You control the entire system from day one.

High-security locks cost more up front — typically two to four times a standard deadbolt — but the long-term control and reduced rekeying costs often justify the investment. For commercial clients, the ROI is clear when you factor in liability and the cost of a security breach.

How to Get High-Security Keys Duplicated (The Right Way)

When you install a restricted keyway system, your locksmith will issue a key control card with a unique serial number. Keep this card secure — it's your proof of ownership.

To get a duplicate, bring the original key and the card to an authorized locksmith. They'll verify the card matches their records, confirm you're authorized, and cut the key on the spot. Some systems allow you to designate multiple cardholders, so a building manager or property owner can both authorize duplicates.

Blanks are serialized in some systems, creating an audit trail. If a key turns up unauthorized, the manufacturer can trace it back to the issuing locksmith. This accountability discourages rogue duplication.

Lost your key card? Contact the locksmith who installed the system. They may require additional ID or proof of ownership — a deed, lease agreement, or utility bill showing you control the property. It's a hassle by design; that friction is what keeps unauthorized users out.

High-Security Keys vs. Do Not Duplicate Stamps

You've probably seen keys stamped "Do Not Duplicate." It's a request, not a legal restriction. Any retail clerk can ignore the stamp — there's no law preventing them from cutting a copy if they have the blank.

A restricted keyway is enforceable. The blank physically doesn't exist in the retail supply chain, so no amount of asking or demanding will produce a duplicate. The patent and distribution controls are legal mechanisms, not just a polite suggestion.

If security matters, don't rely on a stamp. Upgrade to a patented keyway. The difference is real and measurable.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get a high-security key copied at a hardware store?

No. High-security keys use patented keyway designs and restricted blanks that aren't sold to retail stores or kiosks. Only authorized locksmiths with factory credentials can order the blanks and cut duplicates.

How much does it cost to duplicate a restricted key?

Expect to pay $25 to $75 per key, depending on the brand and complexity. You'll also need to present your key control card or authorization form to an authorized locksmith.

What happens if I lose my key control card?

Contact the locksmith who installed your system. You'll need to provide proof of property ownership — a deed, lease, or utility bill. The locksmith may re-issue a card or verify your identity through the manufacturer's records before cutting duplicates.

Are high-security locks worth the cost for a home?

If you've had contractors, previous owners, or housekeepers with keys, upgrading to a restricted keyway ensures no old copies work and no one can duplicate your keys without permission. The up-front cost is higher, but long-term control and peace of mind often justify it.

Can a locksmith bypass or pick a high-security lock?

A skilled locksmith with specialized tools can open most locks in an emergency, but high-security locks are significantly more resistant to picking, bumping, and drilling than standard pin-tumbler locks. They're designed to meet ANSI/BHMA Grade 1 standards for forced entry resistance.
