Door Lock Jammed or Key Won't Turn? Causes and Fixes

Published June 27, 2026

Why Your Key Won't Turn: The Most Common Causes

A key that inserts but won't rotate usually points to one of four problems. Pin-tumbler locks rely on precise alignment—if pins stick, the plug can't turn. Dirt, graphite buildup, and corrosion gum up the chamber over time. Cold weather can make metal contract and lubricants thicken, especially in exterior deadbolts.

Worn keys are another frequent culprit. Brass keys lose their crisp edges after years of use. If you have a copy made from a copy, tolerances stack up and the new key may not lift pins correctly. A bent key will bind inside the keyway before it reaches full insertion.

Misalignment happens when a door sags on its hinges or the strike plate shifts. The bolt tries to extend into a hole that's no longer centered, creating back-pressure that locks the cylinder. You'll feel resistance as you turn, then nothing. Finally, internal parts—springs, driver pins, or the tailpiece—can break or seize, especially in cheaper locks or very old hardware.

Safe DIY Fixes You Can Try Right Now

Start with lubrication. Spray a Teflon or silicone-based lock lubricant into the keyway—never WD-40 or oil, which attract dust. Insert and remove the key several times to work the lubricant into the pin chambers. Graphite powder is a traditional fix, but modern dry lubes are cleaner and just as effective.

Check your key for visible wear or bends. Hold it flat against a new key or a straight edge. If the cuts look rounded or the blade is warped, try your spare. If the spare works smoothly, the problem is the key, not the lock. Most hardware stores and locksmiths can cut a fresh key from your original in minutes.

Inspect door alignment. Close the door and watch the deadbolt as you extend it without the key turned all the way. If it scrapes the strike plate or misses the hole, the door has settled. Tightening hinge screws often brings everything back into line. If the top hinge is loose, the door drops and the bolt aims high. You can also slightly enlarge the strike-plate hole with a file as a temporary fix, but that's less secure long-term.

Wiggle and apply gentle pressure. Insert the key fully, then pull the door toward you or push it away while you turn. Sometimes the bolt is under tension from the door frame, and a little shift releases it. Don't force the key—broken keys stuck in cylinders are a common emergency call.

When the Lock Itself Is the Problem

If lubrication and a good key don't help, the issue is inside the cylinder. Pin-tumbler locks use spring-loaded pins that must align at the shear line. A stuck pin—rusted, bent, or jammed with debris—prevents rotation even when the key is correct. Wafer locks, common in older file cabinets and some residential doors, have similar failure modes but are generally easier to replace than repair.

Frozen locks are an outdoor winter issue. Moisture enters the keyway, freezes overnight, and binds the pins. A few seconds of gentle heat from a lighter or hair dryer on the key (not the lock body) can thaw it. De-icing sprays work, but they're just alcohol and lubricant—often not worth the markup.

Internal breaks are harder to spot. If you feel no resistance and the key spins freely, the tailpiece or cam that connects the cylinder to the bolt mechanism has sheared. This happens in cheap locks or after years of heavy use. The lock must be disassembled and the part replaced. On mortise locks and some commercial-grade deadbolts, a skilled locksmith can swap the cylinder without replacing the entire assembly, saving you money.

Lockout Scenarios: What to Do If You're Stuck Outside

If you're locked out and the key won't turn, don't panic. First, confirm you're using the right key—if you have multiple similar keys, it's easy to grab the wrong one. Check all other entry points: back doors, garage entries, sliding doors. Many people forget they left a window unlatched.

Avoid YouTube lock-picking tutorials. Even if you own the property, amateur attempts with improvised tools usually damage the lock and make a locksmith's job harder and more expensive. Drilling, bumping, or using a broken key extractor without training can wreck the cylinder entirely.

Preventing Jammed Locks: Maintenance Tips

Lubricate your locks twice a year—once before winter, once in spring. A quick spray and a few key insertions keep pins moving freely. If you live near the ocean, do it quarterly; salt air accelerates corrosion.

Replace worn keys before they fail. If your key looks visibly rounded or you've been using the same copy for five years, cut a fresh one from the original. Store your original in a safe place and use copies for daily carry. This preserves the master profile and ensures you always have a reference for future duplicates.

Tighten door hinges annually. A door that sags even a quarter inch will stress the lock and bolt. Use 3-inch screws in at least one hole per hinge—they bite into the wall stud, not just the jamb, and stop sag before it starts.

Upgrade old or builder-grade locks. If your deadbolt is more than ten years old or rates below ANSI/BHMA Grade 2, consider replacement. Modern locks have better tolerances, corrosion-resistant finishes, and pick-resistant pin stacks. A locksmith can recommend options that fit your door prep and security needs without a full hardware swap.

When to Call a Locksmith Instead of DIY

If you've tried lubrication, checked your key, and adjusted the door with no luck, the lock likely needs professional service. Internal damage, broken springs, and seized pins require disassembly and parts you won't have on hand. Attempting to force the cylinder can snap the key off inside, turning a simple jam into a full lock replacement.

High-security locks—those with sidebar mechanisms, restricted keyways, or patent-protected designs—should always be serviced by a pro. These locks are engineered to resist picking and drilling, which also means DIY repairs are nearly impossible without specialized tools and training.

Smart locks add another layer of complexity. If your electronic deadbolt won't turn, the motor, clutch, or battery contacts may have failed. Forcing a jammed smart lock can break the motorized actuator, a repair that often costs as much as a new unit. A locksmith can test the mechanism, revert it to manual mode if needed, or reprogram the controller without voiding your warranty.

Commercial and master-keyed systems require licensed service. Rekeying or replacing one cylinder in a master-key hierarchy affects every other lock on that system. Only a locksmith with your keying schedule and access to the correct key blanks can maintain system integrity. Mistakes here compromise building security and can lock out employees or tenants.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does my key go in but won't turn in the lock?

The most common causes are dirty or corroded pins inside the cylinder, a worn or bent key, or misalignment between the bolt and strike plate due to a sagging door. Lubrication and checking your spare key are good first steps.

Can I use WD-40 to fix a stuck lock?

No. WD-40 and oil-based lubricants attract dust and grime, which will make the problem worse over time. Use a dry Teflon or silicone-based lock lubricant, or traditional graphite powder.

What should I do if my key is stuck and won't turn or come out?

Don't force it. Spray lubricant into the keyway, wiggle the key gently while pulling, and try turning it slowly. If it won't budge, call a locksmith—forcing a stuck key often breaks it off inside the lock.

How do I know if I need to replace the lock or just rekey it?

If the lock mechanism is damaged, badly corroded, or very old, replacement is often smarter. If the lock works but you just need new keys or the cylinder is worn, rekeying is faster and cheaper. A locksmith can assess on-site.

Will a locksmith damage my door to get me in?

A skilled locksmith uses non-destructive techniques first—picking, bypassing, or decoding the lock. Drilling is a last resort and done precisely to minimize damage. Reputable locksmiths will explain options and costs before starting any destructive work.
