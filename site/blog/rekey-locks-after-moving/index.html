Just Moved In? Why You Should Rekey Your Locks Right Away

Published June 13, 2026

Who Has Keys to Your New Home?

The moment you close on a house or sign a lease, you receive a set of keys. But you're rarely the only one who has them.

Previous owners, their family members, contractors, real-estate agents, property managers, house sitters, cleaners, and neighbors may all have copies. In some cases, keys were handed out years ago and never returned. You have no way to know how many duplicates are floating around.

Even if the seller hands you "all the keys," that's no guarantee. Copies are cheap and easy to make. A single unreturned key is a security risk you can't afford to ignore.

Rekey vs. Replace: What's the Difference?

Changing locks means removing the entire lock cylinder and installing a new one. Rekeying means keeping the existing hardware and altering the internal pins so old keys no longer work. Both achieve the same security goal, but rekeying is faster and cheaper.

Most pin-tumbler deadbolts and knob locks can be rekeyed in minutes. A locksmith removes the cylinder, swaps out the pin stack to match a new key, and reassembles. You get fresh keys, the old ones are useless, and your hardware stays in place.

Replace locks if they're damaged, outdated, or low-grade. If you want to upgrade to ANSI Grade 1 deadbolts or add smart locks, replacement makes sense. Otherwise, rekeying is the practical move for most new homeowners.

When Rekeying Isn't Enough

Some situations call for full lock replacement. If the deadbolt is a builder-grade lock with a flimsy strike plate, upgrade it. If the door has no deadbolt at all, add one. If locks are visibly worn, rusted, or sticky, replace them.

Smart locks and keyless entry systems require new hardware. If you're switching from mechanical keys to a keypad or app-based system, you'll need to replace the cylinder entirely.

Check every exterior door, including side doors, basement entries, and garage access doors. Many homeowners rekey the front door and forget the back. Every entry point needs the same level of security.

Do Renters Need to Rekey?

Yes, if your landlord allows it. Most leases don't prohibit rekeying, but you may need to provide the landlord with a copy of the new key.

Apartment turnover is high. The previous tenant, their guests, maintenance staff, and property management all had access. Rekeying gives you control over who can enter your space.

If your lease forbids lock changes, ask the landlord to rekey before you move in. Some will do it automatically; others need a request. If they refuse and you're uncomfortable, consider adding a secondary lock like a door bar or portable deadbolt for added security when you're inside.

What About Garage Door Openers and Gate Codes?

Locks aren't the only access points. If your new home has a garage, reprogram the opener immediately. Most systems let you reset the code in minutes using the control panel.

Same for gate keypads, alarm systems, and smart home hubs. Change every default code and password. If the previous owner used a simple PIN or left the system on factory settings, you're vulnerable.

Check mailbox locks, too. USPS cluster boxes use standardized keys, but individual mailbox locks can be rekeyed or replaced if you're concerned about mail theft.

How to Rekey Your Locks

If you're handy and the locks are standard pin-tumbler cylinders, you can buy a rekeying kit and do it yourself. You'll need the kit, the existing key, and patience. Follow the instructions carefully — one misaligned pin and the lock won't turn.

For most people, hiring a locksmith is faster and safer. A mobile locksmith comes to your home, rekeys every lock to match a single new key, and tests each one before leaving. No guesswork, no mistakes.

Professional rekeying typically costs less than replacing all your locks. You get immediate peace of mind and avoid the hassle of coordinating multiple trips to the hardware store.

Other Security Steps for New Homeowners

Rekeying is the first step, not the last. Inspect every door frame for weak spots. Reinforce strike plates with 3-inch screws that anchor into the wall stud, not just the trim. A solid deadbolt is useless if the strike plate rips out with a single kick.

Check window locks. Many homes have old single-hung windows with flimsy latches. Add secondary locks or window bars if you're on the ground floor.

Install motion-sensor lights at entry points. Trim bushes and trees that provide cover near doors and windows. Visible security measures deter opportunistic break-ins.

Finally, get to know your neighbors. They're your first line of defense when you're away. A friendly relationship means someone notices when something looks off.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to rekey locks after moving?

Rekeying typically costs $20-$50 per lock, depending on the lock type and your location. Rekeying multiple locks at once usually comes with a discount. It's cheaper than replacing all your hardware.

Can I rekey locks myself?

Yes, if you buy a rekeying kit and follow the instructions carefully. However, most homeowners find it faster and safer to hire a locksmith, especially if you have multiple locks or unfamiliar lock types.

Should I rekey or replace my locks?

Rekey if your locks are in good condition and you just want to invalidate old keys. Replace if locks are damaged, low-grade, or you want to upgrade to smart locks or higher-security deadbolts.

Do all locks need to be rekeyed at once?

Yes, rekey every exterior door lock on the same day. If you stagger it, old keys remain active on some doors, defeating the purpose. A locksmith can rekey all locks to match a single new key.

What if I'm renting — can I still rekey?

Most leases allow rekeying as long as you provide the landlord with a copy of the new key. Check your lease or ask property management before making changes.
