Locked Out of Your Apartment? Renter's Guide to Getting Back In

Published June 26, 2026

First Steps: What to Do Right Now

Stop. Take a breath. Being locked out is frustrating, but panic won't help. Check your pockets and bag one more time—keys turn up in odd places when you're stressed.

If you're certain the keys are inside or lost, assess the situation. Are you locked out during business hours or at 2 a.m.? Is your landlord local or out of state? Do you have a roommate with a spare key nearby? Your answers determine your next move.

If it's an emergency—severe weather, a child inside, or a safety issue—call 911 first. For standard lockouts, you have options. Most renters should start by contacting their property manager or landlord. If that's not possible or practical, a licensed mobile locksmith can get you in quickly. When to Call Your Landlord or Property Manager

Check your lease. Many rental agreements require you to contact the landlord or property management office before hiring outside help. Some landlords provide after-hours emergency numbers specifically for lockouts.

If it's during office hours and your management company is nearby, call them first. They may have a master key or spare on file. Larger complexes often have maintenance staff who can let you in at no charge or for a small fee—usually much less than a locksmith.

The downside: response time. If your landlord is unavailable, doesn't answer, or can't arrive for hours, you may need a faster solution. You're not required to wait indefinitely, especially in unsafe conditions or extreme temperatures.

Document everything. Note the time you called, who you spoke with, and any delays. If you hire a locksmith after a reasonable attempt to reach your landlord, keep the receipt. Some landlords reimburse lockout costs if you followed proper protocol.

When to Call a Locksmith Instead

If your landlord is unreachable, out of town, or can't respond within a reasonable timeframe, call a licensed locksmith. Mobile locksmiths arrive fast—often within 30 minutes—and can open most apartment doors without damage.

Common scenarios where a locksmith makes sense: late-night lockouts when the office is closed, landlords who don't keep spares, or situations where you need in now. A professional can pick or bypass a standard pin-tumbler lock in minutes, or rekey the lock on the spot if your keys are lost.

Before any work starts, ask for a quote. Reputable locksmiths provide up-front pricing with no hidden fees. If the price seems inflated or the locksmith won't quote over the phone, hang up and call someone else.

One important note: never hire a locksmith to bypass a lock you don't legally occupy. Locksmiths verify ownership or lease status. Bring your ID and lease agreement if possible, or be ready to show mail with your name and address.

Your Legal Rights as a Renter

You have the right to access your rental unit. Landlords cannot lock you out as retaliation or to force payment—that's illegal in most states and called a "self-help eviction." If your landlord changed the locks without notice, contact a tenant rights attorney immediately.

If you hire a locksmith because your landlord was unavailable, check your local tenant laws. In many jurisdictions, landlords must reimburse reasonable lockout costs if they failed to provide access within a reasonable time. "Reasonable" varies by state, but generally means a few hours during the day, longer at night.

Keep all receipts and records. If your landlord refuses to reimburse and you believe they violated your right to access, small claims court is an option. Most cases settle when renters show they made a good-faith effort to contact the landlord first.

Some leases include clauses that make tenants responsible for lockout fees. Read yours carefully. Even with such a clause, you're entitled to safe, timely access—especially in emergencies.

What It Costs and How to Avoid Surprise Charges

Locksmith pricing for apartment lockouts typically ranges from $75 to $150 during business hours, higher after-hours or on weekends. The cost depends on lock type, time of day, and whether rekeying is needed.

Standard pin-tumbler deadbolts and knob locks are straightforward. High-security locks, smart locks, or mortise locks take longer and cost more. If your keys are lost—not just locked inside—rekeying the lock adds $20 to $50 per lock to prevent unauthorized access.

Avoid scams: never hire a locksmith who won't provide a phone quote, arrive in an unmarked van, or demands cash only. Legitimate locksmiths are licensed, insured, and quote prices before starting work. If the on-site quote jumps dramatically from the phone estimate without clear reason, you can refuse service.

Landlord-provided lockout services are often cheaper or free, but slower. Weigh cost against urgency. If you're locked out in extreme heat with a pet inside, speed matters more than saving $100.

How to Prevent Future Apartment Lockouts

Give a spare key to someone you trust—a nearby friend, family member, or coworker. Hide-a-key rocks and fake sprinkler heads are easy to find; a trusted person is safer.

Some renters use keypad deadbolts or smart locks. Check your lease first—many landlords prohibit tenants from changing locks without permission. If allowed, a keypad lock eliminates the need for a physical key. Install it yourself or hire a locksmith to ensure proper backset and bore sizing.

Keep your landlord's after-hours contact info saved in your phone. Add the property management emergency line to your favorites. When you need it, you won't have to search.

Consider a small lockbox mounted discreetly near your door, with a spare key inside. Again, get landlord approval in writing. Lockboxes with resettable codes are more secure than keyed versions.

If you lose keys frequently, attach a Bluetooth tracker. It won't prevent a lockout if the keys are inside, but it helps you locate them faster when they're misplaced.
