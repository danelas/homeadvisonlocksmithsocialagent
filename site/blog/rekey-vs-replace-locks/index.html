Rekey vs. Replace Locks: Which Do You Need?

Published June 12, 2026

What Rekeying Actually Means

Rekeying reconfigures the pin-tumbler mechanism inside your existing lock cylinder so that a new key operates it and the old keys stop working. A locksmith removes the cylinder, swaps out the pins to match a different key cut, and reassembles everything. The hardware stays on your door.

Most pin-tumbler deadbolts and knob locks can be rekeyed in minutes. Schlage, Kwikset, Yale, and similar residential brands use standard pin stacks that make the process straightforward. High-security cylinders like Medeco or Mul-T-Lock can also be rekeyed, though they require specialized pins and may cost more.

Rekeying does not improve security if your lock is already worn, loose, or low-grade. It only changes which key opens it.

When Rekeying Makes Sense

Choose rekeying when your existing hardware is in good shape and you simply need to control who has access. Common situations include:

  • Moving into a new home. You don't know who has copies of the old keys—previous owners, contractors, house sitters.
  • Lost keys. If you've misplaced a set and worry someone could find them, rekeying stops those keys from working.
  • Ending a relationship or roommate arrangement. Rekeying is faster and cheaper than replacing every lock.
  • Consolidating keys. A locksmith can rekey all your locks to operate on a single key, so you carry one instead of a ring full.

If your deadbolt turns smoothly, the strike plate is secure, and the lock meets ANSI/BHMA Grade 2 or better, rekeying is usually the smartest move. You get fresh security without the cost of new hardware.

When You Should Replace Instead

Replace your locks when the hardware itself is the weak point. Signs you need new locks:

  • Damage or wear. If the cylinder is corroded, the bolt sticks, or the keyway is visibly worn, rekeying won't fix mechanical problems.
  • Low-grade hardware. Builder-grade locks often carry ANSI/BHMA Grade 3 ratings—minimal security. Upgrading to Grade 1 deadbolts with hardened strike plates and longer throw bolts adds real protection.
  • Outdated technology. If you want smart locks, keypad entry, or Bluetooth control, you need new hardware. Rekeying doesn't add features.
  • Non-rekeyable locks. Some cheap or proprietary locks cannot be rekeyed cost-effectively. Wafer locks and certain imported cylinders may be cheaper to replace outright.

Replacing also makes sense if you're already upgrading door hardware for aesthetic reasons. Match your new knobs and deadbolts to your home's style while improving security at the same time.

Cost Comparison: Rekeying vs. Replacing

Rekeying is almost always less expensive. A mobile locksmith typically charges per lock to rekey—often less than half the cost of buying and installing new hardware. If you have five exterior doors, rekeying all of them to one key is a budget-friendly option.

Replacing locks means paying for the new lockset plus installation labor. Quality deadbolts from brands like Schlage or Kwikset run anywhere from mid-range to premium depending on finish and security rating. Add installation time, and the total climbs quickly.

That said, if your locks are already failing or you need a security upgrade, the extra cost of replacement is justified. A Grade 1 deadbolt with a reinforced strike plate resists kick-ins far better than an old Grade 3 lock with fresh pins.

Can You Rekey Locks Yourself?

DIY rekeying kits exist for common brands like Kwikset and Schlage. They include new pins, a follower tool, and instructions. If you're comfortable with small mechanical tasks and own standard pin-tumbler locks, you can attempt it.

Risks include losing springs or pins, damaging the cylinder, or misaligning pins so the lock jams. If you make a mistake, you may need a locksmith to fix it anyway—adding cost and frustration.

For most homeowners, a professional rekey is faster, guaranteed to work, and covers multiple locks in one visit. Master Keying and Advanced Options

If you manage a rental property, small business, or multi-unit building, ask about master keying. This system lets you carry one master key that opens every lock, while tenants or employees get individual keys that only open their assigned doors.

Master keying requires additional pin stacks inside each cylinder. It's more complex than standard rekeying and must be done by a professional locksmith. The payoff is streamlined access control without sacrificing security.

For residential clients, we can set up sub-master systems—for example, one key for all exterior doors and a separate key for interior rooms. This adds convenience without handing out dozens of keys.

Making the Right Choice for Your Home

Start by inspecting your current locks. Turn the key—does it glide or grind? Check the deadbolt throw; a Grade 1 lock extends at least one inch into the door frame. Look at the strike plate: is it secured with short screws into thin trim, or long screws into the wall stud?

If everything works well and you just need to invalidate old keys, rekeying is the clear winner. If your hardware is flimsy, damaged, or you want to add smart features, replacement makes sense.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to rekey a lock compared to replacing it?

Rekeying typically costs less than half the price of buying and installing a new lock. Exact pricing depends on lock type and quantity, but rekeying is almost always the budget-friendly option if your hardware is in good condition.

Can all locks be rekeyed?

Most pin-tumbler deadbolts and knob locks from major brands like Schlage, Kwikset, and Yale can be rekeyed. Some cheap or proprietary locks, and certain wafer locks, may be more cost-effective to replace than rekey.

How long does rekeying take?

A professional locksmith can rekey a standard pin-tumbler lock in minutes. Rekeying an entire home with multiple doors usually takes less than an hour, depending on the number of locks and any complications.

Will rekeying make my locks more secure?

Rekeying changes which key opens the lock but does not improve physical security. If your lock is low-grade or damaged, upgrading to a higher ANSI/BHMA-rated deadbolt with a reinforced strike plate is a better investment.

Should I rekey or replace locks after moving into a new house?

If the existing locks are solid and work smoothly, rekeying is the smart choice—it's faster and cheaper. Replace them only if they're worn, low-quality, or you want to upgrade to smart locks or better security hardware.
