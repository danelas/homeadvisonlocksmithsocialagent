Stop Door Kick-Ins: The Strike Plate Upgrade That Works

Published June 24, 2026

Why Doors Get Kicked In (And Why Your Lock Isn't the Problem)

When someone kicks a locked door, the deadbolt itself rarely fails. The wood around the strike plate—the metal plate screwed into your door frame—splinters and gives way. Most factory strike plates are held by two half-inch screws that barely reach past the trim. One good kick and the frame splits.

The deadbolt is doing its job. The frame isn't. Burglars know this. They target the jamb, not the lock cylinder. A Grade 1 ANSI-rated deadbolt mounted to a weak strike plate offers almost no real protection.

The fix is simple and permanent: upgrade the strike plate and anchor it deep into the wall framing. This turns your door frame into a structural barrier that absorbs and distributes kick force across solid lumber, not surface trim.

What Makes a Strike Plate Actually Strong

Not all strike plates are equal. A proper security strike plate has three characteristics:

  • Heavy-gauge steel construction — 16-gauge or thicker, not stamped sheet metal
  • A box design or deep pocket — fully encloses the deadbolt throw so it can't be pried or jimmied out
  • Mounting holes for 3-inch screws — long enough to reach through the jamb, through the trim, and into the wall stud behind

The screws are the critical part. Three-inch screws penetrate the solid framing lumber—usually a 2x4 stud—and create a connection point far stronger than the door frame itself. When someone kicks, the force travels into structural wood, not decorative molding.

Many hardware stores carry reinforced strike plates. Look for models labeled for ANSI/BHMA Grade 1 deadbolts. They cost between ten and thirty dollars and install in about twenty minutes with a drill and a chisel.

How to Install a Reinforced Strike Plate

Remove the old strike plate and deadbolt. Open the door and locate the existing screw holes in the jamb. You'll likely see two short screws holding a thin plate.

Unscrew the old plate and set the new reinforced strike plate in position. The box or pocket should align with the hole where your deadbolt extends. Mark the new screw holes with a pencil.

Use a drill bit slightly smaller than your screw diameter to create pilot holes. Drill straight in—aim for the wall stud behind the jamb. If you hit solid wood resistance about two inches in, you've found the stud. If the bit spins freely, adjust your angle slightly up or down until you catch framing lumber.

Drive 3-inch wood screws through the strike plate, through the jamb, and into the stud. Tighten firmly but don't overtighten—you can crack the jamb if you strip the threads. Test the deadbolt. It should slide smoothly into the box. If it binds, use a wood chisel to deepen the mortise slightly until the bolt seats fully.

Beyond the Strike Plate: Full Door Reinforcement

A strong strike plate is the single most effective upgrade, but a truly hardened door addresses three points of failure: the strike-side jamb, the hinge-side jamb, and the door itself.

Hinge-side reinforcement: Replace at least one screw in each hinge with a 3-inch screw that reaches the stud. If someone tries to kick the hinge side or pry the door outward, the hinges hold fast.

Door edge reinforcement: A steel wrap or edge guard reinforces the bolt pocket in the door itself. Hollow-core doors and older solid doors can crack around the bolt hole under repeated kicks. Edge guards prevent this.

Frame reinforcement kits: Steel jamb sleeves or wrap plates cover the full length of the strike-side jamb. These distribute force across a wider area and are especially useful for doors with damaged or repaired frames.

For maximum protection, combine all three. The result is a door assembly that resists not just casual kicks but sustained, determined attacks. Most burglars move on after one or two failed attempts—they want fast entry, not a construction project.

Does This Work on Metal or Fiberglass Doors?

Yes. Metal and fiberglass doors are already more kick-resistant than hollow wood, but the frame is still the weak point. The same strike plate upgrade applies—the frame material and stud connection matter more than the door slab itself.

Fiberglass doors often come with compression weatherstripping that makes the bolt-to-strike alignment tight. When you install a deeper box strike plate, test the bolt throw carefully. You may need to adjust the strike position up or down by a sixteenth of an inch to ensure smooth operation.

Metal door frames in commercial settings sometimes use masonry anchors instead of wood screws. If your frame is steel set in concrete, use tapcon or sleeve anchors rated for the frame thickness. What If Your Frame Is Already Damaged?

If previous kick attempts or forced entry have split the jamb, a strike plate alone won't hold. The wood has lost structural integrity. You have two repair options:

Jamb reinforcement sleeves: Steel or aluminum channels that wrap the damaged section and redistribute load to undamaged wood above and below the split. These install over the existing jamb and accept the strike plate on the exterior face.

Jamb replacement: Cut out the damaged section and sister in a new piece of lumber, properly secured to the stud. This is a carpentry job—if the damage is severe, call a locksmith or contractor who can rebuild the frame correctly.

Do not rely on wood filler or epoxy alone. These materials don't have the shear strength to resist kick force. They'll hold a door closed under normal use but fail immediately under impact.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will a reinforced strike plate work with my existing deadbolt?

Yes. Reinforced strike plates are compatible with standard ANSI-sized deadbolts. The upgrade happens on the frame side—you keep your current lock and key.

How long does a strike plate upgrade take?

Most DIY installs take 20-30 minutes with basic tools. A professional locksmith can complete the job in under 15 minutes, including alignment checks and frame inspection.

Can I install a reinforced strike plate on an apartment door?

Check your lease first. Most landlords allow or even encourage security upgrades that don't damage the door. Use screws that match existing holes when possible, and keep the original hardware to reinstall when you move out.

What if I can't find the wall stud behind my door frame?

Use a stud finder or tap along the jamb—solid wood sounds dull, hollow cavities sound hollow. If the stud is off-center, angle your pilot holes toward it. A locksmith has the tools to locate framing quickly and drill accurate pilot holes every time.

Do I need to reinforce both the deadbolt and the knob strike plates?

Reinforce the deadbolt first—it's the primary security lock. Knob locks offer minimal resistance and are easy to force even with a strong strike plate. Focus your effort where it matters most.
