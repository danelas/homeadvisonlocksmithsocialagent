Key Broke Off in the Lock? Here's the Fix

Published June 12, 2026

What to Do Right Now

First: don't force anything. If you keep turning the cylinder or pushing the key fragment deeper, you'll jam the pins and make professional extraction harder—and more expensive.

Pull out any visible portion with needle-nose pliers if you can grip it firmly. If the break is flush with the keyway or recessed, stop. You need extraction tools or a locksmith.

Do not spray lubricant yet. WD-40 or graphite can push metal shavings deeper into the pin chambers and gum up the mechanism. Clean extraction first, lubrication after if needed.

If you're locked out and this is your only key, call a mobile locksmith. If you have a spare and can wait, you have time to try a careful DIY approach or schedule a locksmith service call at your convenience.

Why Keys Break Inside Locks

Most breaks happen because the key was already weakened. Worn grooves, micro-cracks from years of use, or a cheap duplicate cut on soft brass all create stress points. When you turn hard—especially in cold weather when metal contracts—the key snaps at the thinnest spot, usually near the bow or at a deep cut.

Pin-tumbler deadbolts are common culprits. If the lock is stiff or the pins are corroded, turning torque spikes. The key gives before the cylinder does.

Other causes: a stuck latch that won't retract, a misaligned strike plate forcing you to wiggle the key, or internal debris (dirt, old graphite, metal filings from a bad rekey). Any resistance you fight with extra force increases break risk.

Safe DIY Extraction Methods

If part of the key sticks out, grip it with needle-nose pliers at the base—closest to the keyway. Pull straight out in line with the plug. Don't twist. If it doesn't come easily, stop.

If the break is flush or recessed, you need a broken-key extractor set (sold at hardware stores for under $10). These are thin, hooked metal strips. Slide one along the top of the key blade, rotate slightly to catch the cuts, and pull straight out. You may need to turn the cylinder to the at-rest position first—pins should not be binding the fragment.

Another trick: use two small flathead screwdrivers or stiff wire pieces, one on each side of the key, to pinch and walk the fragment out. Patience wins. Jerking or prying will push it deeper.

If the key won't budge after two or three careful attempts, you risk damaging the lock internals. Call a professional. A mobile locksmith has specialized hook tools, plug followers, and can rekey or replace the cylinder on-site if extraction fails.

When to Call a Locksmith Immediately

Call right away if you're locked out with no spare key. A locksmith can extract the fragment, pick or disassemble the lock if needed, and cut a new key from the broken pieces or the lock code—all in one visit.

Also call if the key broke off deep in a high-security lock (Medeco, Mul-T-Lock, ASSA). These cylinders have sidebars, secondary cuts, or restrictive keyways that make DIY extraction nearly impossible without damaging the mechanism.

If you've already tried and pushed the fragment in further, stop. A locksmith can still help, but the job is now more complex. The longer you dig, the higher the chance you'll need a full cylinder replacement instead of a simple extraction.

For automotive locks or ignition cylinders, never attempt DIY extraction. Transponder programming and wafer-lock extraction require tools you don't have. One wrong move and you're looking at a tow and dealership visit. A mobile automotive locksmith can extract and cut a chip key on-site for far less.

How Locksmiths Extract Broken Keys

A locksmith will first assess the break position and cylinder type. Most residential pin-tumbler deadbolts can be extracted in minutes with a hook tool or spiral extractor—a threaded wire that screws into the key fragment and pulls it out.

If the key is wedged or the pins are binding, the locksmith may disassemble the lock, remove the cylinder plug, and push the fragment out from behind. This is faster than fighting a jammed keyway and doesn't damage the lock.

For high-security or automotive locks, extraction may involve shimming, plug rotation with a special tension tool, or—if the fragment is too deteriorated—drilling out the lock core and replacing it. A reputable locksmith will explain your options and quote the cost before starting work.

After extraction, the locksmith can cut a new key from the broken pieces or from the lock's factory code if available. If you want to rekey while the cylinder is already out, that's the ideal time—saves a second service call.

Preventing Future Key Breaks

Replace worn keys before they snap. If the grooves are shiny, the edges rounded, or you see hairline cracks, get a fresh duplicate cut from the original or a high-quality blank. Cheap hardware-store copies use softer brass and wear faster.

Keep locks lubricated. Use dry graphite powder or a Teflon-based lock spray (not WD-40) once or twice a year. Graphite won't attract dirt. Spray it into the keyway, insert and remove the key a few times to distribute it, then wipe excess.

If your lock is stiff, don't force it. Stiffness means the pins are corroded, the keyway is dirty, or the latch is misaligned. A locksmith can clean and rekey the cylinder or adjust the strike plate. Forcing a key in a sticky lock is the fastest way to snap it.

Avoid leaving keys in outdoor locks in freezing weather. Metal contracts, and if moisture gets in the keyway and freezes, turning torque spikes. If you must use an outdoor lock in winter, cover the keyway or use a de-icer spray designed for locks—not a lighter or torch, which can warp the cylinder.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use super glue to pull out a broken key?

No. Glue will seep into the pin chambers and keyway, permanently jamming the lock. You'll need a full cylinder replacement instead of a simple extraction. Use mechanical tools only.

Will a locksmith need to replace the whole lock if the key broke inside?

Usually not. Most broken keys can be extracted without damage. If the fragment pushed pins out of alignment or the cylinder is already worn, the locksmith may recommend rekeying or replacing just the cylinder—not the entire lockset.

How much does broken key extraction cost?

Typically $75–$150 for a standard residential lock, depending on complexity and time of day. High-security or automotive extractions may cost more. Reputable locksmiths quote before starting work.

Can I make a new key from the broken pieces?

Yes, if you have both halves. A locksmith can measure the cuts and duplicate a new key. If you only have one piece or the key is too worn, the locksmith can decode the lock cylinder or use the factory key code if available.

What if the key broke in my car ignition?

Stop trying to extract it yourself—ignition cylinders have wafer tumblers and often electronic components. Call a mobile automotive locksmith who can extract the fragment and program a new transponder key on-site.
