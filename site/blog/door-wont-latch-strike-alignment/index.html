Door Won't Latch? It's Probably the Strike Plate

Published June 30, 2026

Why Doors Stop Latching (And Why It's Not the Lock)

When a door won't stay closed, most people assume the lock is broken. It's almost never the lock. The latch bolt itself works fine—it just can't reach the strike plate hole in the frame.

Three common causes: wood frame movement from humidity or settling; loose hinge screws letting the door sag; loose strike plate screws letting the plate shift. All three change the geometry by millimeters. That's enough to miss.

Inspect the strike plate while the door is open. Close the door slowly and watch where the latch bolt lands on the frame. If it's hitting metal instead of sliding into the hole, you've confirmed misalignment.

The Fast DIY Fix: Tighten and Test

Start with the easiest fix. Grab a screwdriver.

Step one: Tighten every hinge screw on the door. If the top hinge was loose, the door sagged and the latch dropped below the strike hole. Use 3-inch screws in at least one hole per hinge—they bite into the stud behind the trim and pull everything square.

Step two: Tighten the two strike plate screws. If the plate had rotated even slightly, the latch can't enter cleanly.

Step three: Test the latch. Close the door gently and see if it catches. If it latches now, you're done. If it still misses, move to strike plate adjustment.

When You Need to Move the Strike Plate

If tightening didn't work, measure the gap. Close the door until the latch touches the frame and look at the offset. Usually it's 1/8 inch up, down, or sideways.

You have two options: file the existing hole larger (works for small shifts), or move the entire plate (better for anything over 1/8 inch).

To file: Remove the strike plate, clamp it in a vise, and use a metal file to elongate the hole in the direction the latch needs to go. Reinstall and test. This is clean and fast for minor misalignment.

To move the plate: Unscrew it, fill the old screw holes with wood filler or toothpicks and glue, mark the new position with the door closed and latch extended, chisel a new mortise if needed, drill new pilot holes, and reinstall. Use 3-inch screws into the stud—short screws only grab trim and will pull out under stress. Moving a strike plate correctly takes 20 minutes and a sharp chisel. Doing it poorly leaves a weak frame that fails again in weeks.

Deadbolts That Won't Latch: Same Problem, Higher Stakes

Everything above applies to deadbolts, but the consequences are worse. A passage latch that misses is annoying. A deadbolt that won't throw leaves your door unsecured.

Deadbolt strike plates take more force—every time you turn the key or thumb-turn, you're driving a 1-inch bolt into the frame. If the hole is even slightly off, the bolt binds or stops halfway. Over time, forcing it damages the bolt mechanism or cracks the strike plate.

Check the strike box depth. ANSI Grade 1 deadbolts need a strike box at least 1 inch deep, screwed through the jamb into the stud. Surface-mount strikes with 3/4-inch screws into trim are cosmetic. They won't stop a kick. If you're adjusting the strike anyway, upgrade to a reinforced box strike with 3-inch screws. It's the single biggest security improvement you can make to a wood-frame door.

Warped Doors and Seasonal Movement

Sometimes the door itself warped. Solid-core wood doors swell in summer humidity and contract in winter. If your door latches fine in January and misses in July, the door has moved—not the frame.

Exterior doors see the most movement because one side faces weather and the other faces climate control. A 1/4-inch warp across a 36-inch door is enough to throw the latch off.

Short-term fix: Adjust the strike plate to the widest position that still catches year-round. Long-term fix: Plane or sand the door edge in the binding area, or replace the door with fiberglass or steel, which don't warp. Hollow-core interior doors rarely warp but can twist if hinges loosen. Tighten the hinges first before assuming warp.

When the Frame Is Out of Square

If the door was installed in a rough opening that wasn't plumb, or if the house has settled unevenly, the entire frame may be racked. Tightening screws won't fix geometry.

Test with a level on the hinge-side jamb. If it's more than 1/4 inch out of plumb over 7 feet, the frame is skewed. The door will latch at the top or bottom but not both, or it will bind on the hinge side while gapping on the latch side.

Fixing a racked frame means removing trim, shimming the jamb back to plumb, and reinstalling. Not a locksmith job—this is carpentry. But a locksmith can tell you if that's the real problem before you waste time filing strike plates that will never align.

Why Pros Carry Strike Plate Shims and Longer Screws

A mobile locksmith fixing a latch problem on-site carries strike plate shims—thin brass or steel washers that go behind the plate to move it forward without re-mortising the jamb. Add a 1/16-inch shim and the latch catches. It's faster than chiseling and just as strong if you use proper screws.

Longer screws are non-negotiable. The 3/4-inch screws that come with most strike plates bite into pine door trim, which is soft and splits easily. A 3-inch screw goes through the trim, through the jamb, and into the 2×4 or 2×6 stud behind it. That's what stops a kick. Every strike plate we install or adjust gets at least one 3-inch screw. It's the difference between security hardware and decoration.

If you've tightened, filed, and shimmed and the door still won't latch reliably, call a locksmith. The problem might be a worn latch mechanism, a bent bolt, or a frame issue that needs a carpenter. Frequently Asked Questions

Can I fix a door that won't latch without tools?

Not really. You'll need at least a screwdriver to tighten hinge and strike plate screws. If that doesn't work, you'll need a drill, chisel, file, or wood filler to adjust the strike plate position.

How do I know if the problem is the strike plate or the lock itself?

Close the door slowly and watch where the latch bolt hits the frame. If it's hitting wood or metal instead of sliding into the strike plate hole, it's alignment. If the latch doesn't extend when you turn the knob, the lock mechanism is broken.

Why does my door latch in winter but not summer?

Wood doors swell in humidity. A solid-core exterior door can gain 1/8 to 1/4 inch across the width in summer, enough to shift the latch out of alignment. Adjust the strike plate or plane the door edge.

Do I need to replace the whole strike plate or just adjust it?

Usually adjust. If the plate is bent, cracked, or surface-mount only, replace it with a reinforced box strike and 3-inch screws. Otherwise, file the hole larger or move the plate to match the new latch position.
