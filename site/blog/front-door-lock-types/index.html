Front Door Lock Types: Choosing the Right Lock for Your Home

Published June 29, 2026

Why Your Front Door Lock Choice Matters

Your front door is the primary entry point for most break-ins. The lock you install is your first line of defense. A weak knob-only setup invites forced entry. A Grade 1 deadbolt with a reinforced strike plate stops most intruders cold.

Lock types differ in mechanism, strength, and convenience. Some use pin-tumbler cylinders; others rely on wafer tumblers or electronic credentials. Some mount inside the door (mortise locks); others surface-mount. Each has trade-offs in security, cost, and installation complexity.

This guide walks you through the most common residential front-door lock types so you can match your security needs to the right hardware.

Deadbolts: The Security Standard

Deadbolts are the gold standard for front doors. Unlike spring-latch knobs, a deadbolt's solid metal bolt extends deep into the door frame — typically one inch or more. The bolt doesn't retract with pressure; you need a key or thumb-turn to move it.

Single-cylinder deadbolts use a key outside and a thumb-turn inside. Double-cylinder deadbolts require a key on both sides, adding security if your door has glass panels nearby. Just remember: double-cylinder locks can slow emergency exit, so check local fire codes before installing.

Look for ANSI/BHMA Grade 1 or Grade 2 deadbolts. Grade 1 withstands ten strike tests at 75 foot-pounds and 250,000 lock cycles — serious durability. Grade 2 is cheaper but still resists casual forced entry. Pair any deadbolt with a three-inch strike-plate screw that anchors into the wall stud, not just the thin door jamb.

Most deadbolts use pin-tumbler cylinders, which a licensed locksmith can rekey quickly if you lose a key or move into a new home. Rekeying costs far less than replacing the entire lock.

Knobs and Levers: Convenience, Not Security

Doorknobs and levers use spring-latch mechanisms. Turn the knob; the latch retracts. Release it; a spring pushes the latch back into the strike. Simple and cheap, but the latch is short and angled — a credit card or shim can defeat many in seconds.

Knobs work fine for interior doors or secondary entries. For your front door, treat them as convenience hardware only. Pair every knob or lever with a separate deadbolt above or below. Never rely on a knob alone for exterior security.

Levers are easier to operate than knobs — no grip strength needed. ADA guidelines recommend levers in commercial and multi-family buildings. If you have arthritis or limited hand mobility, a lever plus deadbolt is a smart combo.

Wafer-tumbler knobs are common in builder-grade homes. They're cheaper than pin-tumbler but easier to pick. If you're upgrading, swap to a pin-tumbler knob or just remove the knob and install a deadbolt with a handleset instead.

Handlesets: Style Meets Function

A handleset combines a decorative exterior handle with an interior lever or knob, plus an integrated or separate deadbolt. You see these on upscale homes — they look elegant and provide solid security when the deadbolt is Grade 1 or 2.

Handlesets come in two styles: two-point and three-point. Two-point locks secure at the deadbolt and latch. Three-point locks add a third anchor at the top or bottom of the door, spreading force across multiple points. Three-point systems are overkill for most single-family homes but useful on tall or heavy doors.

Installation requires precise boring — one hole for the deadbolt, another for the latch, and careful alignment so the handle sits level. If you're replacing an old knob, the existing holes may not line up. A locksmith can drill new holes, patch old ones, or recommend a handleset that fits your current backset and bore size.

Finishes matter. Match your handleset to your door hardware — oil-rubbed bronze, satin nickel, polished brass. Most manufacturers offer keyed-alike sets so one key opens both the deadbolt and any side or back doors.

Smart Locks: Keyless Entry and Remote Control

Smart locks replace or supplement traditional deadbolts with electronic credentials: keypads, fingerprint scanners, Bluetooth, or Wi-Fi apps. No physical key required — unlock with a code, your phone, or biometric data.

Most retrofit smart locks mount over your existing deadbolt's interior side. The outside cylinder stays; inside, a motor turns the thumb-turn. You keep a key backup in case batteries die. Some models integrate directly with home automation systems like Alexa, Google Home, or Apple HomeKit.

Battery life varies. Expect six months to a year on four AA batteries, depending on usage. Low-battery warnings give you weeks of notice. Always keep spare batteries on hand.

Smart locks use the same ANSI/BHMA grading as mechanical locks. A Grade 1 smart deadbolt is as strong as a traditional Grade 1 — the electronics don't weaken the bolt. Check that the lock's backset (the distance from door edge to cylinder center) and bore diameter match your door. Standard is 2⅜-inch or 2¾-inch backset and 2⅛-inch bore.

One caution: some budget smart locks store Wi-Fi credentials insecurely or lack encryption. Stick with known brands that publish security audits. If you're unsure which model fits your door, call a mobile locksmith for a free consultation before you buy.

Mortise Locks: Heavy-Duty Commercial-Grade Security

Mortise locks mount inside a pocket (mortise) cut into the door's edge. They're bulkier and stronger than cylindrical deadbolts. A single mortise lock can combine latch, deadbolt, and even a night-latch in one unit.

You'll find mortise locks on older homes, apartment buildings, and commercial storefronts. They're expensive and require professional installation — cutting a mortise weakens the door unless it's solid wood or metal. Modern fiberglass and hollow-core doors often can't support a mortise without reinforcement.

Mortise locks offer excellent durability. Many last decades with minimal maintenance. They accept interchangeable cores (IC cores), making rekeying instant if you have the control key. Property managers love this feature — one master key system across hundreds of units.

If your home already has a mortise lock, keep it. Replacing with a cylindrical deadbolt leaves a large hole you'll need to patch and refinish. A locksmith can rekey or rebuild the mortise lock instead.

Choosing the Right Lock for Your Front Door

Start with security. A Grade 1 or Grade 2 deadbolt is non-negotiable for front doors. Add a reinforced strike plate with three-inch screws. If your door has glass within 40 inches of the lock, consider a double-cylinder deadbolt or a smart lock that alerts you to tampering.

Match the lock to your door's construction. Solid wood and steel doors handle any lock type. Fiberglass doors need careful boring to avoid cracking. Hollow-core doors are too weak for most deadbolts — upgrade the door first.

Measure your backset and bore before buying. Most residential locks use a 2⅜-inch backset and 2⅛-inch bore, but older doors vary. If your measurements don't match standard sizes, a locksmith can re-drill or find a compatible lock.

Consider convenience. If you have kids who lose keys, a smart lock with codes makes sense. If you prefer traditional keys, a keyed-alike set for all exterior doors saves keyring clutter. If you're renting, ask your landlord before installing a smart lock — some leases prohibit modifications.

Budget matters but don't cheap out on your front door. A quality deadbolt costs $50–150. A handleset runs $150–400. Smart locks range $100–300. Installation by a professional adds $75–200 but ensures correct alignment and warranty coverage. DIY is possible if you're handy, but one mistake drilling the bore can ruin a $400 door.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most secure type of front door lock?

A Grade 1 deadbolt with a one-inch throw and a reinforced strike plate anchored into the door frame's stud provides the highest residential security. Pair it with a solid-core door for maximum protection.

Can I install a smart lock on any front door?

Most smart locks retrofit over standard deadbolt holes with a 2⅜-inch or 2¾-inch backset. Measure your door's backset and bore diameter first. If the measurements don't match, a locksmith can re-drill or suggest a compatible model.

Should I use a single-cylinder or double-cylinder deadbolt?

Single-cylinder deadbolts (key outside, thumb-turn inside) are fastest for emergency exit. Double-cylinder deadbolts (key both sides) add security if your door has glass panels within reach of the lock, but check local fire codes before installing.

How often should I replace my front door lock?

Replace locks when you move into a new home, lose a key, or after a break-in. Otherwise, a quality deadbolt lasts 10–15 years. Rekeying is cheaper than replacing if the lock body is still in good shape.

Are handlesets more secure than standalone deadbolts?

A handleset is only as secure as its deadbolt component. Look for a Grade 1 or Grade 2 deadbolt integrated into the handleset. The decorative handle and latch add convenience but not meaningful security on their own.
