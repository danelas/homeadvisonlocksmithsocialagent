Why the Fake Rock Doesn't Work — Smarter Spare-Key Options

Published June 25, 2026

Why Fake Rocks, Mats, and Planters Are the Worst Hiding Spots

Burglars know where you hide your spare key. Under the doormat, in a fake rock by the walkway, above the doorframe, inside the mailbox—these are the first places a thief checks. Industry studies show that forced-entry burglaries often involve no force at all; criminals simply use a hidden key.

Fake rocks stand out. They look newer than real landscaping stones, feel lighter when kicked, and sit in obvious spots near the front door. Porch plants and flower pots shift when someone lifts them, leaving scuff marks and dry soil rings. Door-frame ledges collect dust except where a key rests. Even magnetic hide-a-key boxes stuck under a car bumper or behind a mailbox flag are easy to spot once someone knows to look.

Every time you hand a key to a dog walker, house sitter, or repair tech, you're trusting they won't remember where it's stashed. Former contractors, old neighbors, and disgruntled ex-employees have all used hidden keys to enter homes months or years later. The convenience isn't worth the risk.

Wall-Mount Lockboxes: The Contractor Standard

Real estate agents and property managers rely on wall-mount lockboxes for a reason—they work. A quality lockbox bolts to an exterior wall, fence post, or garage doorframe with tamper-resistant screws. You set a four-digit code, place your spare key inside, and only people who know the combination can open it.

Look for lockboxes with hardened-steel shackles and weather-sealed compartments. Cheap models with sliding covers or flimsy plastic bodies can be pried open with a screwdriver in seconds. ANSI-rated products resist cutting, prying, and weather corrosion. Mount the box somewhere visible but not directly next to your main entry—side door, back gate, or garage side wall—so you can monitor it from inside or via a security camera.

Change the combination every few months, and whenever you grant temporary access to a contractor or guest. Write the code in your phone's encrypted notes app, not on a sticky note in your wallet. If you lose phone access, a trusted neighbor with the code becomes your backup.

Give a Key to a Trusted Neighbor (But Do It Right)

A reliable neighbor with a spare key beats any hiding spot. Choose someone who's home often, stays in regular contact, and lives close enough to help in an emergency. Avoid neighbors you barely know or who have high tenant turnover.

Label the key so they know which door it opens—front, back, or garage side. Store it in a labeled envelope in their home, not loose in a junk drawer where it might get lost. Let them know you'll call or text if you need it; don't assume they'll remember in a panic. Offer to hold a spare for them in return; reciprocity builds trust.

Update your neighbor if you rekey your locks after a break-in, lost key, or contractor job. An old key left with someone who doesn't know it's outdated creates a false sense of security. For more on when rekeying makes sense, visit our locksmith blog.

Smart Locks and Keypad Deadbolts: No Physical Key Needed

Keypad deadbolts and smart locks eliminate the spare-key problem entirely. You enter a four- to eight-digit code on a backlit touchpad instead of fumbling for metal. Most models install in a standard deadbolt bore (2-1/8 inch diameter, 2-3/4 inch backset) and use AA batteries that last six to twelve months.

Smart locks connect to your phone via Bluetooth or Wi-Fi. Grant temporary access codes to guests, dog walkers, or cleaners—then delete the code when the job ends. You see a log of who unlocked your door and when. Some models integrate with Ring, Google Home, or Alexa for voice control and remote locking if you forget to secure the door on your way out.

Mechanical keypad deadbolts cost less and need no app or internet connection. They're purely code-based—no batteries, no connectivity, no hacking risk. Schlage and Kwikset make residential-grade models that resist weather and wear. Either option is more secure than a hidden key, and you never get locked out as long as you remember your code.

Car Key Lockboxes and Hidden Compartments for Vehicles

Spare car keys present a different challenge. Magnetic hide-a-key boxes under the bumper or wheel well rust, fall off, or get spotted by thieves casing parking lots. Modern transponder keys with chips can't be cut from a photo—they must be programmed to your car's immobilizer—but a hidden mechanical valet key still opens the door.

Portable lockboxes designed for vehicle use attach to trailer hitches, tow hooks, or frame rails with steel cable loops or combination locks. Store only the mechanical door key, not the full fob; that way, someone who finds the box can unlock your car but not start the engine. Keep the programmed fob lockbox at home or in a separate secure location.

Some drivers hide a valet key inside the car—under the floor mat, in the glove box, or behind a removable panel—so a lockout doesn't strand them. This works only if you can access the cabin through a window or sunroof. It does nothing if your key fob dies in a parking lot and you need to start the car. Frequently Asked Questions

Where should I never hide a spare key?

Never hide a key under doormats, in fake rocks near the entry, above doorframes, inside mailboxes, or in obvious planters. These are the first places burglars check and compromise your home security.

Are lockboxes safer than hiding a key under a rock?

Yes. A hardened-steel wall-mount lockbox with a four-digit code resists prying and weather, and you can change the combination. Mount it away from the main door and monitor it with a camera for added security.

Can I hide a spare car key under my vehicle?

Magnetic hide-a-key boxes rust, fall off, and are easily spotted. Use a cable-lock vehicle lockbox attached to a frame rail and store only the mechanical valet key—never the full transponder fob.

Do smart locks work if the batteries die?

Most keypad smart locks include a physical keyhole backup or a 9-volt external power port you can touch to the lock to unlock it temporarily and replace the batteries from inside.

Should I give my spare key to a neighbor or use a lockbox?

Both work. A trusted neighbor offers human judgment and availability; a lockbox gives you sole control over access and lets you change the code without coordinating. Choose based on your relationship and convenience.
