Employee Left? Rekey Your Business the Same Day

Published June 19, 2026

Why Rekeying Matters When Staff Turnover Happens

An unreturned key is a security gap. Even if you trust the person who left, you can't control whether that key was copied, lent out, or lost. Rekeying changes the internal pins of your existing lock so old keys no longer work. You keep the same hardware, save money, and eliminate the risk in under an hour for most commercial deadbolts.

This applies whether someone resigned, was let go, or you're managing seasonal or contract workers. The moment you can't confirm every key is accounted for, it's time to rekey.

Rekey vs. Replace: Which Makes Sense for Your Office?

Rekeying is almost always the smarter play for standard commercial locks. A locksmith removes the lock cylinder, swaps out the pins to match a new key, and reinstalls it—usually in 10 to 20 minutes per lock. Cost is typically a fraction of replacing the entire lockset.

Replace the lock only if it's damaged, outdated, or you want to upgrade security—say, from a Grade 3 residential deadbolt to a Grade 1 commercial-rated lock with a reinforced strike plate. If your existing hardware is solid and the lock still operates smoothly, rekeying is the efficient choice.

One added benefit: rekeying lets you standardize your office to a single master key system. That means one key opens the front door, individual offices, and storage rooms, with optional sub-master keys for managers. Makes key management simpler going forward.

What to Do the Day Someone Leaves

Step one: request all keys back during the exit meeting or final checkout. If you have a key log, verify every copy is returned. If the employee can't locate a key, note it immediately.

Step two: if any key is missing or you have any doubt, schedule a locksmith visit the same day or next business day. Don't wait weeks. The longer the window, the higher the risk.

Step three: make a list of every lock that employee could access—front entrance, side doors, office suites, file cabinets, storage closets. A mobile locksmith can rekey all of them in one trip, usually within a couple of hours depending on how many locks you have.

For urgent situations—terminated employees with access to sensitive areas—24/7 mobile locksmiths can respond after hours or on weekends. You don't have to wait until Monday morning to secure your business.

How Long Does Office Rekeying Take?

Most standard pin-tumbler deadbolts and knob locks are rekeyed in 10 to 20 minutes each. A small office with three or four exterior doors and a handful of interior locks can usually be finished in under two hours, including travel time.

If you have high-security cylinders—Medeco, Mul-T-Lock, or restricted keyways—rekeying still works, but the locksmith needs factory-pinned cores or authorization to cut those keys. That can add lead time if parts need to be ordered, so mention your lock brand when you call.

Electronic locks and smart locks typically don't use traditional pins, so "rekeying" means reprogramming access codes or deleting the former employee's credential from the system. That's often faster than mechanical rekeying—just a few taps in the admin app.

Master Key Systems: Control Access by Role

If you manage multiple employees or departments, a master key system is worth considering during the rekey. Each lock gets its own "change key" that opens only that door. A master key opens every lock in the system. You can add layers—a sub-master for managers that opens their floor, but not the owner's office or server room.

This setup simplifies turnover. When someone leaves, you rekey only the locks they had access to. The master stays the same, and other employees' keys aren't affected. It also reduces the number of keys on your ring and makes it easier to track who has access to what.

A licensed locksmith can design a master key hierarchy that fits your org chart and building layout. Mention it when you call to schedule the rekey.

Cost Factors and What to Expect

Most locksmiths charge a service call fee plus a per-lock rekeying rate. Expect $15 to $35 per lock for standard pin-tumbler cylinders. If you're rekeying five locks, budget around $100 to $200 total, including the trip charge.

Pricing goes up for high-security locks, after-hours service, or if you need new keys cut for the entire office. Ask for an up-front quote before any work starts. Reputable locksmiths will tell you the exact cost over the phone or on-site before touching the locks.

Keep in mind that rekeying is a one-time expense that protects inventory, client files, equipment, and employee safety. Compare that to the potential cost of a break-in or theft enabled by an unreturned key.

Other Security Measures to Pair with Rekeying

While the locksmith is there, consider upgrading strike plates to three-inch screws that anchor into the door frame studs—dramatically improves kick-in resistance. Check that deadbolts have at least a one-inch throw. If you have glass near the door, a double-cylinder deadbolt (key on both sides) prevents someone from breaking the glass and turning the thumbturn.

For offices with sensitive areas—server rooms, cash offices, HR file storage—look at access control keypads or smart locks with audit trails. You'll see exactly who entered and when. No keys to rekey when someone leaves; just delete their code or fob from the system.

If you store valuable equipment or confidential files, ask about commercial-grade filing cabinet locks or high-security padlocks for server racks. Rekeying the front door is step one, but comprehensive security covers every entry point.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I rekey the locks myself?

You can buy a rekeying kit, but it requires removing the cylinder, carefully matching pin heights to the new key, and reassembling without losing springs or pins. One mistake and the lock won't work. For a business, the risk and downtime usually aren't worth the small savings—hire a licensed locksmith.

Do I need to rekey if the employee was trustworthy?

Yes. Even trustworthy former employees can lose a key, lend it to someone, or have it copied without your knowledge. Rekeying closes the gap and is standard practice in professional property management.

How many keys will I get after rekeying?

Most locksmiths include two to three keys per lock in the base price. If you need extras—for managers, cleaning crew, or a spare set in the safe—ask when you schedule. Additional keys usually cost a few dollars each.

Can I rekey just the exterior doors and skip the interior offices?

You can, but if the former employee had a key to interior offices, file rooms, or storage, those remain vulnerable. It's more cost-effective to rekey everything in one visit than to call the locksmith back later.

What if I have electronic locks or keypad entry?

Electronic and smart locks don't use physical keys to rekey, but you can delete the former employee's access code or credential from the system. Most systems let you do this from the admin panel in seconds. If you need help, a locksmith familiar with access control can walk you through it or handle it on-site.
