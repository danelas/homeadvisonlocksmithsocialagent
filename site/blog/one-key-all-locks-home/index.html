Can You Make One Key Open Every Lock in Your Home?

Published June 14, 2026

Yes—and You Have Two Main Routes

Short answer: yes, you can use a single key for all the locks in your home. You have two options: rekey your existing locks so they all match one key, or buy new locksets that are already keyed alike from the factory.

Rekeying is usually cheaper if your hardware is in good shape. A locksmith swaps the pins inside each cylinder so every lock accepts the same key. The door hardware stays put; only the pin stack changes. Takes minutes per lock, no drilling or patching.

Keyed-alike sets—sold as multi-packs at big-box stores—ship with matching keys already. You replace the entire lockset. This makes sense when you're upgrading from builder-grade locks or want a higher ANSI/BHMA security grade at the same time.

How Rekeying Works (and Why It's Usually the Best Option)

Most residential deadbolts and knob-sets use pin-tumbler cylinders. Inside the cylinder, spring-loaded pins sit in chambers. When you insert the correct key, the pins align at a shear line and the cylinder rotates. Insert the wrong key and the pins block rotation.

To rekey, a locksmith removes the cylinder, dumps out the old pins, and replaces them with a new set sized to match your chosen key. The lock body, strike plate, and door hardware stay untouched. As long as your locks use the same keyway (the shape of the key blank), they can be rekeyed to one key—even if they're different brands or finishes.

The process takes 5–10 minutes per lock once the locksmith has the door open. Cost usually runs $15–$30 per lock after a service-call fee, far less than replacing six deadbolts and handles. You keep your existing finish, your doors don't get new holes, and you're done in under an hour for a typical house.

One caveat: if you have a mix of standard pin-tumbler locks and wafer locks (common on sliding doors or some older knob-sets), you may need two keys. Wafer locks use flat tumblers instead of pins, so the internals aren't cross-compatible. A locksmith will spot this during the inspection.

Keyed-Alike Locksets: When to Buy New Instead of Rekey

If your locks are worn, low-grade, or you want a style refresh, buying keyed-alike sets makes sense. Major brands—Kwikset, Schlage, Yale—sell multi-packs (typically two to six locksets) with all locks sharing one key code. You'll see "keyed alike" or "KA" on the box.

This route costs more upfront—expect $100–$300 for a quality set of four to six locks—but you get new hardware, often a jump in security grade (look for ANSI Grade 2 or Grade 1), and a uniform finish. Installation is straightforward if you're handy: remove the old lockset, check that the door's backset and bore match the new lock (standard residential is 2⅜″ or 2¾″ backset, 2⅛″ bore for knobs, 1½″ for deadbolts), and mount the new hardware.

If the existing holes don't line up, you'll need to drill or use a conversion kit. That's where most DIYers call a locksmith—misaligned holes weaken the door and void warranties. A mobile locksmith brings the right jigs and can swap six locks in an hour, ensuring tight fits and proper strike-plate alignment for maximum security.

Master Keying vs Keyed Alike: Know the Difference

Keyed alike means every lock opens with the same single key. One key, six locks, total uniformity. Simple.

Master keying is a tiered system. Each lock has its own change key (so the bedroom lock has one key, the garage another), but a master key opens all of them. Larger homes, rental properties, or offices use this when you want to give different people access to different zones while keeping one override key for yourself.

Master keying requires more pin chambers and careful design—one mistake and a change key can open locks it shouldn't. It's a professional job. If you just want to carry one key for your front door, back door, and deadbolts, keyed-alike is what you need. Save master keying for scenarios where you actually need hierarchical access control.

Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

Mixing brands isn't always a deal-breaker, but keyways differ. Kwikset uses one pin profile, Schlage another. If you have a Kwikset front door and a Schlage back door, a locksmith can often rekey both to a Schlage key (by swapping the Kwikset cylinder for a Schlage-compatible one, or vice versa). Trying to rekey incompatible keyways yourself usually means ordering the wrong pins and wasting an afternoon.

Don't assume every lock in your house is rekeyable. High-security locks with sidebars, magnetic pins, or proprietary keyways (Medeco, Mul-T-Lock, Abloy) require special blanks and tools. If you've upgraded one door to a restricted keyway, that lock may need to stay on its own key, or you rekey the rest to match it—which can be expensive.

Electronic smart locks add another wrinkle. Most have a mechanical-key backup, and that cylinder can be rekeyed to match your other doors. But if the lock is fully keyless, you're managing access through an app instead. You can still have one physical key for the remaining traditional locks.

Finally, cheap locks sometimes use low-quality cylinders that strip easily during rekeying. If a locksmith says a $12 builder-grade knob-set should be replaced rather than rekeyed, listen. Forcing a rekey on marginal hardware can leave you with a lock that binds or fails within months.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I rekey locks myself, or do I need a locksmith?

You can buy a rekey kit for about $15–$30 and do it yourself if you're comfortable disassembling cylinders and have matching keyways. Most homeowners hire a locksmith to avoid mistakes—cross-pinning a lock can leave it open to the wrong key, a serious security risk.

How much does it cost to rekey all the locks in a house?

Expect $100–$200 for four to six locks, including a service call. Rekeying runs $15–$30 per lock after the initial fee. Replacing with new keyed-alike locksets costs $100–$300 for the hardware, plus installation if you hire help.

Will rekeying work if I have different lock brands?

Often, yes—but only if the keyways are compatible. A locksmith can swap cylinders or use universal keyway pins to get most common brands (Kwikset, Schlage, Weiser) on one key. High-security or proprietary keyways may require replacement.

What's the difference between keyed alike and master keyed?

Keyed alike means one key opens all locks—simple and common for homes. Master keyed means each lock has its own key, plus a master key that opens everything—used when you need tiered access control.

Can smart locks be rekeyed to match my other doors?

If the smart lock has a mechanical-key backup cylinder, yes—that cylinder can be rekeyed to match your traditional locks. Fully keyless smart locks don't use physical keys, so you'll manage those separately via app or code.
