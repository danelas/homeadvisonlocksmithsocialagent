Keypad Lock vs. Smart Lock: Which Is Right for You?

Published June 22, 2026

What's the Difference?

Both keypad locks and smart locks let you unlock your door without a metal key. The core difference: keypad locks are standalone devices with a numeric touchpad, while smart locks connect to your phone or home automation system via Bluetooth, Wi-Fi, or Z-Wave.

A keypad lock stores a handful of user codes in its onboard memory. You punch in your code, it checks the code locally, and the deadbolt retracts. No internet, no app, no monthly fees. Popular models include Schlage FE595, Kwikset SmartCode 909, and Yale Assure Lock (non-connected version).

A smart lock adds wireless connectivity. You can lock or unlock from your phone, grant temporary access to guests, see entry logs, and integrate with Alexa, Google Home, or Apple HomeKit. Many smart locks also have a keypad for backup entry. Examples: August Wi-Fi Smart Lock, Yale Assure Lock 2, Schlage Encode, Kwikset Halo.

If you want remote control, activity history, or voice commands, a smart lock is the tool. If you just want to stop carrying keys and skip the complexity, a basic keypad lock does the job.

Price and Installation

Keypad locks start around $80 and run up to $200 for models with higher ANSI/BHMA security ratings (Grade 1 or 2). Smart locks range from $150 to $350, depending on connectivity type and feature set. Wi-Fi models cost more than Bluetooth-only units because they include a built-in bridge; Z-Wave locks require a separate hub like SmartThings or Ring Alarm.

Installation is similar for both: most replace a standard deadbolt. You'll need a 2⅛-inch bore and a 1-inch or adjustable backset (measure your existing lock). Many homeowners swap locks themselves in 20–30 minutes using a screwdriver and the included template. If your door has non-standard prep, a mortise lock, or you're adding a lock to a door that never had one, call a professional. Smart locks chew through batteries faster because radios draw power. Expect four to six months on four AA batteries for a Wi-Fi lock; keypad-only models can last a year or more. Most units beep or flash when batteries run low, and every smart lock includes a physical key override—you won't get locked out if the batteries die.

Security and Lock Grade

Security depends on the lock body, not the input method. Both keypad and smart locks use the same deadbolt mechanisms—typically pin-tumbler cylinders with ANSI/BHMA Grade 1, 2, or 3 ratings. Grade 1 offers the highest resistance to forced entry (ten strikes at 75 foot-pounds, 800,000+ cycles). Grade 2 is residential-strong. Grade 3 is light-duty.

Keypad locks store codes locally in encrypted memory. A well-made unit won't leak your code through side-channel attacks, but cheap models can be vulnerable to brute-force guessing if they don't enforce lockout timers. Look for locks that freeze after a few wrong attempts.

Smart locks add network security questions. Wi-Fi and Bluetooth traffic should be AES-128 or AES-256 encrypted. Reputable brands push firmware updates to patch vulnerabilities; obscure imports may never see an update. Physical tampering alarms—piezo sensors that trigger a siren if someone pries the cover—are common on mid-tier and premium smart locks.

One myth: "Smart locks are easier to hack." In practice, forced entry—kicking the door, prying the strike plate—is far more common than wireless hacking. Reinforce your door frame with a three-inch strike plate anchored into the stud, upgrade to a solid-core door, and either lock type will perform well.

Convenience and Features

Keypad locks win on simplicity. No app to install, no Wi-Fi password to enter, no firmware updates. You program codes using the keypad itself—master code, then user codes. If you need to give a contractor temporary access, you add a code and delete it when the job is done. No phone required.

Smart locks shine when you want remote control. Forgot to lock up? Tap your phone. Need to let a dog walker in at 2 p.m.? Send a time-limited code or unlock remotely. Activity logs show who came and went, with timestamps. Voice control through Alexa or Google Assistant means you can lock the door hands-free while carrying groceries.

Auto-lock and auto-unlock are popular smart-lock features. Auto-lock re-secures the door after 30 seconds. Auto-unlock uses geofencing—your phone's location—to unlock as you approach. Some people love it; others find it creepy or unreliable if Bluetooth range is inconsistent.

Integration with home security systems is another smart-lock advantage. Pair a Yale or Schlage Encode with Ring Alarm, and your system auto-disarms when you unlock. Pair with a video doorbell, and you can see who's at the door before granting access. Keypad locks can't do any of that—they're offline by design.

When a Keypad Lock Makes Sense

Choose a keypad lock if you value simplicity and low maintenance. You're eliminating the hassle of physical keys without adding smart-home complexity. Ideal for rental properties (issue codes instead of cutting keys), back doors, or households where not everyone owns a smartphone.

Keypad locks are also a good fit if you're concerned about cloud dependency. No manufacturer server means no risk of a service outage locking you out—though physical key backups mitigate that risk on smart locks, too.

Battery life is better, total cost of ownership is lower (no hub to buy), and you're not handing activity data to a cloud service. If those priorities matter, stick with a keypad-only model.

When a Smart Lock Makes Sense

Pick a smart lock if you want remote access, guest management, or integration with Alexa, Google, or Apple HomeKit. Parents with teenagers, Airbnb hosts, and anyone who forgets to lock up will appreciate the app control and push notifications.

Smart locks also make sense if you're already invested in a home automation ecosystem. Z-Wave locks pair with SmartThings, Hubitat, or Home Assistant for advanced automation—lock the door, arm the alarm, and turn off the lights in a single routine.

If you value activity logs—knowing exactly when your housekeeper arrived or your kids got home from school—a smart lock records every entry. Keypad locks can't provide that visibility.

Finally, smart locks with built-in cameras or integrated video doorbells (like the Arlo Video Doorbell Lock) give you two-way video and remote unlock in one device. You see the delivery driver, unlock the door, and tell them to leave the package inside—all from your phone.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use a smart lock without a smartphone?

Yes. Most smart locks include a physical keypad and a backup key cylinder, so you can unlock manually even if you don't own a smartphone. You'll lose remote access and app features, but the lock still functions locally.

Do keypad locks work during a power outage?

Yes. Keypad locks and smart locks run on batteries installed inside the door, not household power. A power outage won't affect them at all.

How many user codes can I store?

Keypad locks typically hold 4–19 user codes. Smart locks often allow 100+ codes because they store data in the cloud or on a connected hub, making them better for Airbnb or property management.

Are smart locks compatible with all doors?

Smart locks fit standard deadbolt prep: 2⅛-inch bore, 1-inch backset (some adjust to 2¾ inches). Mortise locks, rim locks, or non-standard doors may need adapters or professional modification.

What happens if I forget my code?

Every keypad and smart lock includes a physical key override. If you lose the key or forget the master code, a locksmith can rekey the cylinder or reset the lock's memory—call us anytime at (786) 777-9529.
