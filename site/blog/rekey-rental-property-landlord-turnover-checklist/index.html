Rekeying Rental Properties: A Landlord's Turnover Checklist

Published July 1, 2026

Why Rekey Between Every Tenant

When a tenant moves out, you have no way to know how many copies of the key exist. Friends, family, contractors, and old roommates might still have access. Rekeying resets that chain of custody.

Many local landlord-tenant laws require you to change locks or provide new keys before a new occupant moves in. Even if your jurisdiction is silent, your lease probably isn't — most standard forms obligate you to deliver exclusive possession. An un-rekeyed lock fails that test.

Rekeying is also cheaper and faster than replacing hardware. A mobile locksmith can rekey a standard pin-tumbler deadbolt and knob in minutes, leaving the same lock body in place. You only swap the internal pins so old keys no longer turn.

Rekey vs. Replace: What Makes Sense

Rekey when the lock cylinder is a standard pin-tumbler or wafer design and the hardware is in good shape. Most residential deadbolts and knob-sets fall into this category. Cost per lock typically runs $20–$40 when done as part of a whole-property visit.

Replace if the lock is damaged, rusted, or uses outdated single-cylinder knobs with no deadbolt. Also replace if you're upgrading to ANSI Grade 1 deadbolts or adding smart locks. A worn strike plate or splintered door frame often means it's time for reinforced hardware, not just new pins.

If you manage multiple units, ask about master key systems. Each apartment gets a unique tenant key, but you carry one master that opens every lock. Timing and Legal Considerations

Rekey after the old tenant returns all keys and before the new tenant gets possession. Some landlords schedule the locksmith for move-out day; others do it during the turnover clean. Either works as long as no overlap exists.

Document the rekey in your turnover checklist. Note the date, which locks were serviced, and how many keys you issued to the new tenant. If a dispute arises later, that paper trail proves you met your duty.

In a few cities, you must use a licensed locksmith and provide a receipt or affidavit to the tenant. Check your local housing code. Even where it's not required, a professional invoice gives you a clean record and ensures the work meets commercial-grade standards.

Never rekey while a tenant still holds lawful possession — that's an illegal lockout in most jurisdictions. Wait for the lease to end, keys to be returned, or a court-ordered eviction to be final.

Master Key Systems for Multi-Unit Properties

A master key system lets you open every unit with one key while each tenant's key works only their door. Larger systems add layers: a sub-master for each building or floor, and a grand master for the entire complex.

Setting up a master system requires factory keyways or compatible blanks. Not every lock can be mastered, so if your property has a mix of old hardware, the locksmith may recommend replacing a few cylinders to standardize the keyway.

Keep your master key secure. If it's lost, you'll need to rekey every lock in the system — an expensive lesson. Some landlords store a duplicate in a safe or with their property manager rather than carrying it daily.

Master systems simplify maintenance and emergency access but do create a single point of failure. Weigh convenience against the risk, and always disclose to tenants that a master key exists if your lease or local law requires it.

Common Mistakes Landlords Make

Skipping the rekey to save $30. That decision can cost thousands if an unauthorized person enters and damages the unit or harms the new tenant. Liability aside, it's usually a lease breach.

Using the same key code across all units. It's tempting for convenience, but if one tenant loses a key, every apartment is compromised. Unique keys per unit, paired with a master for you, is the safer route.

Forgetting exterior doors and common areas. Mailbox locks, laundry rooms, storage units, and pool gates also need attention during turnover. An old tenant with a laundry-room key still has building access.

DIY rekeying without the right pins. Hardware-store rekey kits work for homeowners who own the lock long-term, but landlords juggling multiple units and key schedules benefit from professional speed and the ability to integrate a master system. What to Hand the New Tenant

Provide at least two keys per lock — one for the tenant, one spare. If roommates are on the lease, give each their own set. Mark keys with the unit number or a code, never the street address.

Walk the tenant through which key opens which lock: front door, back door, mailbox, storage. Test every key in their presence so there's no confusion on move-in day.

Include the locksmith receipt or a note in the lease packet confirming that locks were rekeyed on a specific date. This proves the keys are fresh and that prior occupants no longer have access.

If you installed smart locks or keyless entry, provide the tenant with the access code and instructions for changing it. Even with electronic locks, a physical key override usually exists — rekey or replace that cylinder too.

Budgeting and Scheduling Turnover Rekeying

Plan $50–$100 per unit for standard rekeying (deadbolt and entry knob), more if you're adding a master system or upgrading hardware. Mobile locksmiths often discount multi-unit visits, so if you manage a building, schedule all units the same day.

Call ahead rather than waiting until move-out day. Locksmiths stay busy, especially at month-end when leases turn over. A week's notice usually gets you a convenient time slot. If you have a standing turnover schedule, ask about a service contract for predictable pricing.

Emergency lockouts do happen — a new tenant arrives and the key doesn't work, or you discover mid-showing that the lock is broken. A 24/7 mobile locksmith can solve that in an hour, but you'll pay a premium for same-day service. Routine rekeying scheduled in advance costs less and keeps your turnover on track.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I legally have to rekey between tenants?

Many local codes and standard lease forms require it, and failing to provide exclusive possession can expose you to liability. Check your jurisdiction's landlord-tenant rules and your lease language.

How much does it cost to rekey a rental unit?

Typically $20–$40 per lock for standard pin-tumbler cylinders. A two-lock apartment runs $50–$80. Master key systems and hardware upgrades add to the cost.

Can I rekey locks myself to save money?

Hardware-store kits exist, but professional rekeying is faster, ensures proper pin installation, and integrates master key systems. For landlords managing multiple units, mobile service is usually worth the cost.

What's the difference between rekeying and replacing a lock?

Rekeying swaps the internal pins so old keys no longer work; the lock body stays in place. Replacing means installing entirely new hardware, necessary when locks are damaged or you're upgrading security.

Should I set up a master key system for my rental property?

If you manage multiple units, a master system lets you carry one key while each tenant has a unique key. It simplifies access for maintenance and emergencies but requires careful key control.
