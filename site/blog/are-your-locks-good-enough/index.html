Are Your Locks Actually Good Enough? A 5-Minute Security Check

Published June 24, 2026

Start With Your Deadbolt Grade

Not all deadbolts are equal. The American National Standards Institute (ANSI) and Builders Hardware Manufacturers Association (BHMA) grade locks on a 1-to-3 scale. Grade 1 is highest security — resists 10 strike tests at 75 foot-pounds of force. Grade 3 is basic residential, often found in builder-grade homes.

Check the deadbolt face or packaging for an ANSI/BHMA stamp. If you see Grade 2 or 3, consider upgrading to Grade 1, especially on entry doors. A Grade 1 deadbolt runs $40-$80 retail; installation takes 20 minutes if your door is already prepped with standard backset and bore.

If there's no grade stamp at all, you likely have a generic lock. Replace it. The few dollars saved at install isn't worth the vulnerability.

Inspect Your Strike Plate and Door Frame

The strike plate is the metal pocket screwed into the door frame that catches the deadbolt. Most builder-installed strike plates use half-inch screws that barely grab the frame. A solid kick can rip them out.

Look for a strike plate with at least 3-inch screws that penetrate the wall stud behind the trim. A reinforced box-strike plate — a deeper metal housing — distributes force better than a flat plate. Upgrading a strike plate costs under $15 in materials and is one of the highest-ROI security moves you can make.

Also check the door frame itself. If it's split, rotted, or hollow, even a Grade 1 lock won't help. Frame reinforcement kits exist, or a locksmith can install a wrap-around strike that distributes force across more wood.

Check Your Lock Type: Pin-Tumbler, Wafer, or Smart

Most residential deadbolts use pin-tumbler cylinders — small spring-loaded pins that align when the correct key is inserted. Higher-security pin-tumblers have spool or mushroom pins that resist picking and bumping. Wafer locks, common in older doors and some padlocks, offer less pick resistance.

If you have a smart lock, confirm it has a mechanical backup — battery failure or firmware bugs can lock you out. Check the backset (distance from door edge to lock center, usually 2-3/8" or 2-3/4") and bore size (hole diameter, typically 2-1/8") if you're considering an upgrade. Mismatched dimensions mean re-drilling or adaptors.

For automotive locks, modern vehicles use transponder or chip keys with an embedded RFID that must match the car's immobilizer. Mechanical-only keys are rare after 1995 and far easier to duplicate without permission. If your car key has no chip, consider an upgrade or at minimum keep a spare in a secure location.

Test for Obvious Vulnerabilities

Stand outside each entry door and try this: Can you see the deadbolt latch from outside when the door is locked? If yes, the bolt throw is too short — it should extend at least one inch into the frame. Half-inch throws can be shimmed or forced with a screwdriver.

Check sliding doors and windows. A simple wooden dowel or security bar in the track stops forced entry, but many people forget to use them. Sliding-door locks are often wafer-based and weak; aftermarket locks or a pin through the frame add real security.

Look at door hinges. If they're on the outside of an outswing door, the pins can be popped and the door removed even if locked. Swap to non-removable hinge pins or add a hinge bolt (a pin that interlocks the door and frame when closed).

Evaluate Your Key Control

Do you know who has copies of your keys? If you've lived in your home more than a few years, handed keys to contractors, or bought the property used, someone else likely has access. Rekeying costs $20-$30 per lock and takes minutes — far cheaper than replacing hardware.

Master keying and sub-master systems let you control multiple locks with one key while giving others limited access. Useful for rental properties or businesses, but adds complexity. If you don't need it, stick to single-key systems — fewer pins mean fewer pick points.

Avoid hiding spare keys outdoors. Fake rocks and under-mat spots are the first places checked. A lockbox with a rotating code or a trusted neighbor is safer. Better still, install a keypad deadbolt so you never need a hidden key.

When to Call a Professional

If any lock feels sticky, keys jam, or you have to jiggle the knob to engage the latch, internal pins or springs may be worn. That's a security risk — worn locks are easier to pick or bump. A locksmith can disassemble, clean, and repin the cylinder, or recommend replacement if parts are too degraded.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my deadbolt is Grade 1, 2, or 3?

Check the lock face or original packaging for an ANSI/BHMA grade stamp. Grade 1 is highest security, Grade 3 is basic residential. No stamp usually means a low-quality generic lock worth replacing.

What's the difference between rekeying and replacing a lock?

Rekeying swaps the internal pins so old keys no longer work, but keeps the existing hardware. Replacing installs entirely new locks. Rekeying is faster and cheaper when the lock body is still in good shape.

Can I upgrade my strike plate myself?

Yes, if you're comfortable with a drill. Remove the old plate, install a reinforced box-strike with 3-inch screws into the wall stud, and test the bolt engagement. It's a high-impact, low-cost security upgrade.

Are smart locks as secure as traditional deadbolts?

Quality smart locks with Grade 1 mechanical cylinders and encrypted communication offer comparable security. Always choose models with a physical key backup in case of battery or firmware failure.

How often should I have my locks professionally checked?

Every 3-5 years for standard residential locks, or immediately if you move into a new home, lose a key, or notice sticking or jamming. Regular service catches worn pins and mechanical issues before they become vulnerabilities.
