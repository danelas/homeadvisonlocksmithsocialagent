Deadbolt Grades Explained: Grade 1 vs 2 vs 3

Published June 14, 2026

What Are ANSI/BHMA Deadbolt Grades?

The American National Standards Institute (ANSI) and Builders Hardware Manufacturers Association (BHMA) created a three-tier grading system for deadbolts. Every certified lock passes specific strike tests, cycle tests, and material strength checks. Grade 1 is the toughest. Grade 3 is basic residential protection.

You'll see the grade stamped on the lock packaging or listed in the product specs. Some manufacturers skip certification entirely — those locks have no grade and no independent lab validation. If security matters, stick to graded hardware.

Understanding the differences helps you match the lock to the risk. A front door facing the street needs more protection than a bedroom closet. Budget and threat level both factor in.

Grade 1 Deadbolts: Maximum Security

Grade 1 deadbolts survive ten strike tests at 75 foot-pounds of force — roughly a grown adult kicking hard, repeatedly. The bolt must cycle 800,000 times without failure. The cylinder resists picking and drilling better than lower grades, though no lock is pick-proof.

You'll find Grade 1 locks on commercial buildings, apartment complexes, and homes in high-crime areas. They cost more — usually $100 to $300 installed — but insurance companies sometimes offer discounts for certified Grade 1 hardware on all exterior doors.

Common brands include Medeco, Mul-T-Lock, and Schlage Primus. These often use restricted keyways, meaning you can't duplicate the key at a hardware store without authorization. That limits unauthorized copies.

If you're upgrading to Grade 1, reinforce the strike plate with 3-inch screws driven into the door frame's stud. A strong lock on a weak frame still fails. We install reinforced strike plates as part of commercial-grade deadbolt installations.

Grade 2 Deadbolts: Residential Standard

Grade 2 locks handle five strike tests at 60 foot-pounds and must cycle 400,000 times. Most single-family homes use Grade 2 deadbolts on front and back doors. They balance cost and protection well for typical residential use.

These locks run $50 to $150 installed. Popular models include Kwikset 980, Schlage B60, and Yale B1L. The bolt throw should be at least one inch. Anything less shortens the engagement with the strike plate and makes kick-ins easier.

Grade 2 deadbolts deter opportunistic burglars — most break-ins involve quick, loud force or simple bypasses like unlocked windows. A properly installed Grade 2 deadbolt with a reinforced strike makes forced entry noisy and slow. That's often enough to send someone looking for an easier target.

Grade 3 Deadbolts: Basic Protection

Grade 3 is the minimum ANSI/BHMA standard. One strike test at 40 foot-pounds, 200,000 cycles. These locks cost $20 to $60 and work for low-traffic interior doors or buildings with layered security — gated communities, apartment hallways, or secondary access points behind fences.

Grade 3 deadbolts are better than no deadbolt, but don't rely on them for primary exterior doors. The internal components — springs, pins, cylinders — use lighter-gauge materials. The bolt may be shorter. The strike plate is often thin stamped metal instead of solid brass or steel.

If you're on a tight budget, put a Grade 2 lock on the most vulnerable door (usually the front) and Grade 3 on side or back entries. Then reinforce both strike plates. A $15 lock with a $10 reinforced strike outperforms a $50 lock with factory hardware screwed into hollow trim.

Strike Plates, Backsets, and Installation Details

The deadbolt grade tells half the story. Installation determines the other half. A Grade 1 deadbolt installed with half-inch screws into trim splits the door frame on the first kick. Always use three-inch screws that reach the wall stud. Box strike plates with metal flanges that wrap the jamb add even more strength.

Backset — the distance from the door edge to the center of the cylinder — is usually 2⅜ inches or 2¾ inches. Measure your old lock before buying a replacement. Mismatched backsets mean drilling new holes or leaving exposed voids in the door face.

The bore hole (where the lock body sits) is typically 2⅛ inches in diameter. Smart locks and thicker deadbolts sometimes need larger bores. Check specs before you buy. Enlarging a bore weakens the door unless you use a wrap-around reinforcement plate.

Rekeying vs. Replacing When You Upgrade Grades

Switching from Grade 3 to Grade 2 or Grade 1 usually means replacing the entire deadbolt — the internal mechanisms and cylinder housings differ. Rekeying only swaps the pin-tumbler configuration inside an existing cylinder. It's cheaper and faster, but it doesn't change the lock's grade.

If your current deadbolt is already Grade 1 or Grade 2 and you just want new keys — after a roommate moves out, you buy a house, or you lose a key — rekeying costs $25 to $50 per lock. Most pin-tumbler deadbolts rekey in minutes. We carry blanks for Schlage, Kwikset, Yale, Weiser, and most residential brands on our mobile units.

Smart locks add another layer. Many use mechanical backup cylinders that can be rekeyed separately from the electronic components. If you upgrade to a smart deadbolt, confirm the ANSI/BHMA grade of the mechanical cylinder. Some smart locks use Grade 3 mechanical cores even though the app and touchscreen feel high-tech.

Which Grade Should You Buy?

For most homeowners, Grade 2 deadbolts on all exterior doors offer solid protection at reasonable cost. If you live in a high-crime area, own valuable inventory, or want insurance discounts, step up to Grade 1. Use Grade 3 only for interior doors or secondary entries with multiple security layers already in place.

Commercial properties — retail, offices, warehouses — should default to Grade 1 on perimeter doors. Liability and inventory risk justify the extra cost. Interior office doors can use Grade 2. Storage closets and electrical rooms often get Grade 3 or simple passage locks.

If you're comparing locks at the hardware store, ignore marketing claims about "military-grade" or "unbreakable." Check the packaging for ANSI/BHMA certification and the grade number. That's the only standardized, lab-tested measure you can trust. Everything else is sales copy.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I rekey a Grade 3 deadbolt to make it Grade 1?

No. Rekeying only changes the pin configuration inside the cylinder. The lock's strike resistance, cycle durability, and material strength stay the same. To upgrade grades, you must replace the entire deadbolt.

Do all deadbolts have an ANSI/BHMA grade?

No. Only locks submitted for independent lab testing receive a grade. Many budget locks skip certification. If the package doesn't list a grade, assume it hasn't been tested to any standard.

Is Grade 1 overkill for a typical home?

For most single-family homes in average-crime neighborhoods, Grade 2 is sufficient. Grade 1 makes sense in high-crime areas, for homes with valuable collections, or where insurance discounts offset the extra cost.

How much does it cost to upgrade from Grade 3 to Grade 2?

A Grade 2 deadbolt with professional installation and a reinforced strike plate typically runs $75 to $150 per door. DIY installation saves labor but requires accurate backset and bore measurements.

Do smart locks come in Grade 1?

Some do. August, Schlage Encode, and Yale Assure offer models with Grade 1 or Grade 2 mechanical cylinders. Always check the ANSI/BHMA rating for the backup keyway, not just the electronic features.
