Smart Lock Installation: What to Know Before You Buy

Published June 21, 2026

Why Door Fit Matters More Than Brand

Smart locks fail at installation for one reason: the door prep doesn't match the lock spec. A $300 smart deadbolt is useless if your backset is 2-3/8" and the lock requires 2-3/4". Returns cost time and money. Measure first.

Three measurements determine compatibility: backset (distance from door edge to center of bore hole), bore diameter (the hole through the door face), and door thickness. Most residential doors are prepped to ANSI standards, but older homes and custom doors vary. Check before you buy.

If you're replacing an existing deadbolt, the new smart lock must match the old hole pattern — or you'll need new drilling. A mobile locksmith can verify fit and handle installation if the prep doesn't align. We measure, advise, and install correctly the first time.

Backset: The Make-or-Break Dimension

Backset is the horizontal distance from the door's edge to the center of the cylinder hole. Standard residential backsets are 2-3/8 inches (common on interior and many exterior doors) or 2-3/4 inches (typical on front entry doors). Measure from the door edge to the center of your current lock hole.

Most smart locks support both backsets with an adjustable latch assembly — but not all. Some budget models ship with a fixed 2-3/8" latch. If your door is 2-3/4", the bolt won't align with the strike plate in the frame. The lock installs but won't latch. Always confirm the product spec lists both backsets or matches yours exactly.

Commercial doors sometimes use a 5-inch backset (mortise lock prep). Standard smart deadbolts won't fit. If you have a mortise lock now — recognizable by a rectangular faceplate on the door edge — you need a mortise-style smart lock or a locksmith to re-prep the door for cylindrical installation.

Bore Diameter and Cross-Bore

The bore is the round hole drilled through the door face where the lock body sits. Standard residential bore diameter is 2-1/8 inches. Measure across the hole with a tape measure or caliper. Most smart locks are designed for this size.

Some older doors have a 1-1/2" bore (common on mid-century interior knobsets). A standard smart deadbolt won't fit without enlarging the hole. A locksmith can drill it out to 2-1/8" cleanly — DIY attempts often splinter the veneer or drill off-center.

The cross-bore (also called latch bore or edge bore) is the smaller hole drilled into the door edge for the latch bolt. Standard is 1" diameter for deadbolts, 7/8" for knobsets. Smart locks follow the same specs, so if you're swapping a mechanical deadbolt for a smart one, the cross-bore usually matches. If it doesn't, the latch won't seat flush and the door won't close properly.

Door Thickness and Clearance

Most smart locks fit doors between 1-3/8 inches and 1-3/4 inches thick. Measure the door itself, not the frame. Interior doors often run 1-3/8"; exterior solid-core or steel doors are typically 1-3/4". Check the product spec sheet — some locks max out at 1-3/4", others accommodate up to 2" with extension kits.

If your door is thicker (custom wood, fire-rated commercial), confirm the smart lock ships with or supports longer mounting screws and extended tailpieces. Installing a lock designed for 1-3/4" on a 2" door leaves the interior and exterior halves loose or misaligned. The lock may power on but won't turn the bolt reliably.

Also verify strike-plate clearance in the door frame. The smart lock's latch bolt must travel fully into the strike without binding. If the frame jamb is shallow or the strike plate is recessed unevenly, the bolt hits wood before latching. A locksmith can deepen the mortise or shim the strike to achieve proper throw.

Handing, Swing, and Exterior Obstructions

Smart locks are usually reversible for left- or right-hand doors, but confirm how the keypad or touchscreen orients. Some models auto-detect handing during setup; others require you to flip internal components. If the instructions are unclear, you may end up with upside-down buttons.

Check door swing and exterior obstacles. If the door swings outward and you have a storm door or screen within 3 inches, the smart lock's exterior escutcheon may collide when both are open. Measure the gap. Low-profile smart locks (under 2" projection from door face) work better in tight vestibules. Bulky motorized deadbolts can interfere.

Interior clearance matters too. Some smart locks have a battery pack or motor housing that sticks out 1.5-2 inches on the inside. If the door opens against a wall or furniture, confirm there's room. We've seen installs where the door hits a baseboard and won't open past 90 degrees because the battery housing is too deep.

Power, Connectivity, and Prep You Can't See

Smart locks run on batteries (usually four AA or a rechargeable lithium pack). No wiring is required for basic keypad or Bluetooth models. Wi-Fi and Z-Wave locks may need a hub or bridge plugged in nearby. Plan where that hub goes before installation day — you don't want the lock installed and discover your router is two floors away with no outlet in range.

If you want hardwired power (rare, mostly commercial), the door must have a conduit channel drilled for low-voltage wiring. Retrofitting this into a residential door is invasive. Battery-powered is simpler and just as reliable — expect 6-12 months per set depending on usage.

For smart locks with auto-lock or geofencing, your phone's Bluetooth or the home Wi-Fi must reach the door. Metal doors and thick frames can block signal. Test connectivity before you drill. If signal is weak, a Wi-Fi bridge or mesh extender solves it. A locksmith familiar with smart lock installation can troubleshoot connectivity on-site and recommend placement.

When to Call a Locksmith for Installation

If your door prep matches the smart lock spec exactly, DIY installation is doable with basic tools — screwdriver, tape measure, drill for pilot holes. Follow the manual step-by-step. But if measurements don't align, don't force it. Drilling a second bore hole off-center weakens the door and voids the lock warranty.

Call a mobile locksmith if:

  • Your backset or bore diameter doesn't match the lock spec and you need re-drilling
  • You have a mortise lock and want to convert to cylindrical smart lock prep
  • The door is steel or fiberglass and you're not confident drilling clean holes
  • The strike plate doesn't align and the bolt won't latch
  • You're installing multiple smart locks on a commercial property and need keying integration

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I install a smart lock on any door?

Most smart locks fit standard residential doors with a 2-1/8" bore, 2-3/8" or 2-3/4" backset, and 1-3/8" to 1-3/4" thickness. Measure your door first — older or custom doors may need re-drilling or a different lock model.

What if my backset doesn't match the smart lock?

You'll need a lock that supports your backset or have a locksmith re-drill the door. Installing a mismatched backset means the bolt won't align with the strike plate and the door won't latch securely.

Do smart locks work on metal or fiberglass doors?

Yes, as long as the door has standard bore and backset prep. Drilling into steel or fiberglass requires sharp bits and care to avoid splintering the surface — a locksmith has the right tools for a clean install.

Can a locksmith install a smart lock if I already bought one?

Absolutely. Bring your smart lock and we'll verify fit, handle any door prep adjustments, install the hardware, and test operation. If the lock doesn't fit your door, we'll recommend compatible alternatives.

How long does smart lock installation take?

On a door with matching prep, installation takes 20-40 minutes. If re-drilling or strike-plate adjustments are needed, plan for an hour. A mobile locksmith completes the job in one visit, no return trips.
