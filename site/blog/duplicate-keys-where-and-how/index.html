Where to Get Keys Duplicated — and Which Keys Can't Be Copied

Published July 1, 2026

Where to Get Standard House Keys Duplicated

Most standard brass house keys can be copied in minutes at hardware stores, big-box home centers, or specialized kiosks. These are typically pin-tumbler or wafer keys with no special markings. Cost runs $2–$5 per copy, and the process is simple: you hand over the original, the machine traces the cuts, and a blank is milled to match.

That said, quality varies. Kiosks and inexperienced clerks sometimes produce keys that bind in the lock or don't turn smoothly. If a freshly cut key doesn't work perfectly, take it back — minor burrs or mis-aligned cuts are the usual culprits. A professional locksmith will test-fit and deburr every key before handing it over.

For any key more complex than a standard residential deadbolt or entry knob key, skip the kiosk and call a licensed locksmith. You'll save time and avoid the frustration of a key that almost works.

High-Security and Restricted Keys

High-security keys carry patents, special keyways, or "Do Not Duplicate" stamps. Brands like Medeco, Mul-T-Lock, and Assa Abloy manufacture keys that cannot be copied without proof of ownership — usually a key card issued when the lock was installed. Hardware stores and kiosks won't touch these, and attempting to grind one on a standard machine will damage both the blank and the cutter.

Why the restriction? High-security locks use sidebar mechanisms, rotating pins, or magnetic elements that standard blanks can't replicate. The keys themselves are milled to tighter tolerances. Copying requires factory blanks and authorization from the original locksmith or lock manufacturer.

If you've lost your key card or bought a home with restricted locks already installed, contact a locksmith with the lock's serial number. Some manufacturers allow authorization transfer; others require a full rekey or lock replacement. Either way, a licensed locksmith can confirm your options and order the correct blanks if you're approved.

Automotive Keys: Transponder Chips and Key Fobs

Car keys made after the mid-1990s almost always contain a transponder chip that communicates with the vehicle's immobilizer. Even if a hardware store can cut the metal blade to fit the ignition, the engine won't start without programming the chip to your car's ECU. That requires specialized equipment a locksmith or dealership has.

Key fobs — remote lock/unlock buttons — add another layer. Some fobs are integrated into the key head; others are separate remotes. Either way, they must be programmed to your vehicle. A mobile locksmith can often do this on-site in 15–30 minutes, depending on the make and model. Dealerships charge more and require an appointment, sometimes keeping your car for hours or overnight.

If you only need a spare metal blade for an older car (pre-1995 or basic fleet vehicles), a hardware store copy works fine. For anything with a chip or fob, save yourself the trip and call a locksmith who can cut, program, and test the key in one visit.

"Do Not Duplicate" Stamps: Are They Legally Binding?

Short answer: no, not in most states. A "Do Not Duplicate" or "Unlawful to Copy" stamp is a deterrent, not a legal restriction. Hardware stores and kiosks often honor the request as a policy courtesy, but they're not legally required to refuse duplication unless the key is patented or part of a registered restricted keyway system.

That said, reputable locksmiths will ask for proof of ownership or authorization before copying a stamped key, especially for commercial or apartment building keys. It's a liability and ethics issue. If you manage a property or business and want real duplication control, upgrade to a patented high-security keyway with a key card system. Stamps alone won't stop a determined tenant or employee from getting a copy made elsewhere.

Keys You Absolutely Cannot Copy Yourself

Patented high-security keys require factory blanks and authorization cards. Transponder car keys need chip programming equipment. Electronic access cards and smart-lock codes aren't physical keys at all — duplication means issuing a new credential through the system's software, which only the administrator or locksmith managing the system can do.

Some older safe keys and antique skeleton keys use non-standard profiles that modern machines can't trace. A locksmith with hand-filing tools can sometimes duplicate these by impression or manual measurement, but it's slow work and not every shop offers it. If you have an unusual key, call ahead and ask if the locksmith has experience with that lock type before driving across town.

Finally, master and sub-master keys in commercial buildings or multi-unit properties should never be copied without property management approval. These keys open multiple doors in a hierarchy; unauthorized duplication is a security risk and often a lease violation. If you're a tenant or employee who needs an extra copy, ask the building manager to issue one through their locksmith.

When to Call a Mobile Locksmith Instead

If you're locked out and need a key made from scratch, a hardware store can't help — you need a locksmith who can pick or disassemble the lock, read the pin depths, and cut a key by code. Mobile locksmiths carry code-cutting machines and blank inventory on their trucks, so they come to you and make the key on-site. No tow truck, no storefront visit.

Same goes for commercial or high-security keys. Walking into a big-box store with a restricted key is a waste of time. A licensed locksmith will verify your authorization, order the blanks if needed, and cut them correctly the first time. You'll also get advice on rekeying or upgrading locks if your security needs have changed.

For standard house keys, hardware stores are fine. For anything more complex — car keys, high-security locks, or emergency lockouts — skip the guesswork and call a professional. You'll save time, money, and the headache of a key that doesn't quite work.

How to Get a Spare Key When You're Locked Out

If you're locked out with no spare, a mobile locksmith can create a new key on the spot. For a standard pin-tumbler deadbolt, the locksmith picks the lock (non-destructively), removes the cylinder, reads the pin stack, and cuts a key by code. The whole process takes 10–20 minutes. For car lockouts, the locksmith uses the VIN and key code databases or impressioning techniques to cut and program a new key.

Some people try to get a key made at a hardware store using a photo of their original key. This almost never works. Key-cutting machines need precise depth and spacing measurements; a photo introduces distortion and guesswork. You'll end up with a key that jams or doesn't turn. If you don't have the original in hand, you need a locksmith with code-cutting capability.

Bottom line: don't wait until you're locked out to get a spare made. If you only have one key to your house or car, get a duplicate cut now while you have time to test it. Stash the spare with a trusted neighbor, in a lockbox, or at your office. It's cheap insurance against a 2 a.m. lockout.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get a high-security key duplicated at a hardware store?

No. High-security keys with patents or restricted keyways require proof of ownership and factory blanks. Only authorized locksmiths or the original installer can duplicate them.

How much does it cost to duplicate a house key?

Standard brass house keys cost $2–$5 per copy at hardware stores. High-security or car keys with chips can run $50–$150 depending on complexity and programming requirements.

Will a locksmith copy a key stamped 'Do Not Duplicate'?

Many will ask for proof of ownership first. The stamp itself isn't legally binding in most states, but reputable locksmiths honor it as an ethical safeguard unless you can verify authorization.

Can I get a car key duplicated without the original?

Yes, if the locksmith has your VIN and access to key code databases. They can cut and program a new transponder key or fob on-site, even if you've lost all originals.

How long does it take to duplicate a key?

Standard house keys take 2–5 minutes. Transponder car keys require 15–30 minutes for cutting and programming. High-security keys may take longer if blanks must be ordered.
