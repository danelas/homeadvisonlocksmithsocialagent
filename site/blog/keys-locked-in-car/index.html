Keys Locked in the Car? Don't Break a Window

Published June 7, 2026

What to Do Right Now

You're locked out. The keys are visible on the seat or in the ignition. Your first instinct might be panic or reaching for a rock. Stop.

Check every door and the trunk. Keyless entry systems sometimes leave one door unlocked. Pop the trunk release if your vehicle has a manual trunk-to-cabin pass-through—some sedans let you fold down the rear seats from inside the trunk.

Look for a spare key. Many drivers keep a magnetic key box under the chassis or leave a spare with a family member. If you have roadside assistance through your insurance or car manufacturer (OnStar, BMW Assist, Mercedes-Benz mbrace), call them. Some plans include remote unlock for newer vehicles with connected services.

If none of those work, call a mobile locksmith. A licensed pro opens the door in minutes without scratching paint or cracking glass. Breaking a window costs $200–$400 for glass alone, plus labor and the security risk of driving with an open window until repair. A locksmith visit costs less and solves the problem cleanly.

How Locksmiths Open Locked Cars Safely

Professional automotive locksmiths use specialized tools designed for non-destructive entry. The most common method is a slim jim or long-reach tool inserted between the window and weather stripping. This manipulates the door lock linkage or pulls the interior handle without forcing anything.

For newer cars with frameless windows or advanced anti-theft systems, locksmiths use an air wedge—a small inflatable bladder that creates a gap at the door's top corner. A rigid tool slides through to press the unlock button or grab the interior handle. The wedge deflates, the door closes flush, and no damage occurs.

Transponder and push-button start vehicles require different techniques. If the key fob is inside, the locksmith may still gain entry with wedge-and-tool, then use the fob to start the car. If you've lost the fob entirely, a mobile locksmith can often program a new transponder key on-site using OBD-II port equipment and factory key codes.

Modern vehicles with proximity sensors or electronic locks sometimes need firmware workarounds. Licensed locksmiths have access to manufacturer databases and programming tools to handle everything from Honda Civic mechanical keys to Tesla Model 3 digital access. The key is calling someone with the right tools and training—DIY attempts with coat hangers or wedges from YouTube risk damaging weather stripping, bending door frames, or tripping alarm systems.

Why Breaking the Window Is a Bad Idea

Desperation makes breaking glass seem logical. It's not. Auto glass replacement runs $200–$450 per window, depending on the make and model. Tempered side glass shatters into hundreds of pebbles that wedge into seats, carpet, door panels, and air vents. You'll be vacuuming fragments for weeks.

Driving with a missing window—even temporarily—exposes the interior to weather and theft. If rain arrives before you get to a glass shop, you're looking at soaked upholstery and potential electrical damage. Insurance may cover the glass under comprehensive, but you'll still pay a deductible and deal with a claim on your record.

Security is another issue. A broken window is an invitation. Even if you tape plastic over the opening, it's obvious the car is compromised. In urban areas, that's a risk you don't need.

A mobile locksmith charges a fraction of glass replacement, arrives in 20–30 minutes in most cases, and leaves your car exactly as it was—locked and intact. The choice is clear.

DIY Methods: What Works and What Doesn't

Internet forums love lockout hacks. Most are ineffective or risky. Here's the reality:

  • String or shoelace loop: Works only on older cars with post-style manual locks (pre-2000 models, mostly). You tie a slipknot, wiggle the string through the door gap, lasso the lock post, and pull. Modern cars have recessed buttons or electronic locks—this method fails.
  • Coat hanger: Requires precision and risks scratching paint or damaging interior linkage. Even if you succeed, you may disable the lock mechanism or trigger the alarm. Not worth it.
  • Tennis ball air pressure trick: A myth. Cutting a hole in a tennis ball and pressing it over the keyhole does nothing. Air pressure doesn't unlock pin-tumbler or wafer locks. Save yourself the mess.
  • Slim jim (purchased online): Professional slim jims are calibrated for specific makes and models. Generic ones sold to consumers often bend, break, or catch on airbag wiring. You can cause hundreds in electrical damage trying to save fifty bucks.

If you absolutely must attempt entry yourself, use an inflatable wedge (sold at auto parts stores) and a plastic pry tool to press the unlock button—but only if you know your car's layout and are willing to accept responsibility for any damage. Honestly, calling a pro is faster and safer.

What About Roadside Assistance?

Many drivers have lockout coverage through AAA, insurance (Geico Emergency Roadside Service, State Farm), or manufacturer programs. These services are valuable, but know the limits.

Response times vary. During peak hours or in remote areas, you may wait 45 minutes to two hours. Roadside contractors are generalists—they jump batteries, change tires, and unlock doors, but they're not always specialist automotive locksmiths. If your car has a complex electronic lock or transponder system, the roadside tech may not have the tools to help.

Coverage caps matter. Some plans cover only basic lockout service. If you need a new key programmed or a door lock repaired after a failed DIY attempt, you'll pay out of pocket. Check your policy details before you're stranded.

A dedicated mobile locksmith often arrives faster, brings specialized tools for your exact vehicle, and can handle everything from a simple unlock to on-site key fob programming. If you're not enrolled in roadside assistance or you've used your annual service limit, a locksmith is the direct solution.

Preventing Future Lockouts

Once is enough. A few simple habits keep you from repeating this scenario:

  • Hide a spare key. Magnetic key boxes attach under the chassis near the rear bumper. Use one designed for outdoor exposure (weather-sealed). Store only a mechanical door key, not a transponder fob—losing a $300 fob to a car wash or theft isn't worth it.
  • Leave a spare with someone you trust. A family member or neighbor who's usually home can be a lifeline. Just make sure they know where the key is.
  • Use a keyless entry code if your car has it. Many Ford, Lincoln, and some Nissan models have door keypads. Memorize the code and you'll never lock yourself out again.
  • Keep your phone in your pocket, not the car. If your keys are in the ignition and you step out to grab something from the trunk, your phone is your only link to help if the door locks behind you. Make it a habit: phone and wallet stay on your person.
  • Consider a Bluetooth key tracker. Tile or AirTag attached to your keyring can ping your phone if you can't find your keys—and some apps alert you if you walk away from your keys. Won't prevent a lockout, but it helps with general key management.

When to Call a Locksmith Immediately

Some lockouts are more urgent than others. Call a mobile locksmith right away if:

  • A child or pet is locked inside. Even on a mild day, car interiors heat up fast. A 75°F outside temperature can hit 100°F inside in ten minutes. Call 911 first, then a locksmith. First responders may break a window—better that than heatstroke.
  • You're in an unsafe location. Roadside, parking garage, unfamiliar neighborhood at night—don't wait around. A locksmith gets you mobile again quickly.
  • The car is running with keys inside. Unlocked cars left running are theft magnets. Get help fast.
  • You've already tried DIY and caused damage. Bent door frames, broken handles, triggered alarms—stop before it gets worse. A locksmith can assess and repair on the spot or advise on next steps.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a locksmith charge to unlock a car?

Most mobile locksmiths charge $50–$100 for a standard car lockout during business hours, with higher rates after hours or for complex electronic locks. Always ask for a quote before service begins to avoid surprises.

Can a locksmith open my car without damaging it?

Yes. Licensed locksmiths use slim jims, air wedges, and long-reach tools designed for non-destructive entry. Damage is extremely rare when a trained professional does the work.

How long does it take for a locksmith to unlock a car?

Once on-site, most locksmiths unlock a car in 5–15 minutes. Response time varies by location and traffic, but mobile locksmiths typically arrive within 20–30 minutes of your call.

Will my car insurance cover a locksmith for a lockout?

Some comprehensive or roadside assistance policies cover lockout service. Check your policy or call your insurer. Even if not covered, a locksmith is usually cheaper than breaking a window and filing a glass claim.

What if I lost my key fob and I'm locked out?

A mobile locksmith can gain entry and program a new transponder key or fob on-site for most vehicles. This requires access to your VIN and proof of ownership, and costs more than a simple unlock, but you'll drive away the same day.
