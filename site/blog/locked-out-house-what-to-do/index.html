Locked Out of Your House? Do This First

Published June 6, 2026

Stop and Take Stock—Do Not Panic

You're locked out. You're frustrated. Maybe it's late. Before you grab a hammer or try to climb through a window, pause for sixty seconds.

Most house lockouts have a safe, quick solution that doesn't involve broken glass or a trip to urgent care. Assess the situation: Are you alone? Is it an emergency (pet inside, stove on)? Do you have your phone? Knowing what you're dealing with helps you pick the smartest next step.

If you're in immediate danger—weather, health issue, or someone inside needs help—call 911 first. Otherwise, follow the steps below.

Check Every Door and Window (But Stay Safe)

Walk your perimeter. Many homes have a back door, side door, or garage entry you forgot to lock. Try every knob. If you have a sliding glass door, check that the latch didn't just stick.

Windows are trickier. Ground-floor windows sometimes get left unlocked, but never force a window or screen—you can shatter glass, bend frames, or fall. If a window is open and you can reach it safely from the ground without climbing, fine. If not, skip it. A $150 locksmith call beats a $3,000 ER bill.

Garage doors with keypads are gold if you remember the code. If your garage is attached and the interior door is unlocked, you're in. If the garage door opener is in your car and the car is in the driveway, you might still be stuck—but check anyway.

Do You Have a Spare Key Somewhere?

Think hard. Did you leave a key with a neighbor, family member, or friend nearby? Is there a lockbox on the property (realtor box, hidden key safe)? Some people tape a spare under a deck rail or inside a grill cover. Not ideal for security, but useful now.

Do not look under the doormat, fake rock, or planter if you're in a high-traffic area and someone is watching. You're broadcasting where you hide keys. If you do retrieve a hidden spare, move it to a better spot or give it to a trusted neighbor later.

If a friend or family member has a key but they're an hour away, decide if waiting is realistic. If it's 2 a.m. or you have kids, pets, or medication inside, call a locksmith instead. Time matters.

When You Should Call a Locksmith Right Now

If none of the above worked, a professional locksmith is your fastest, safest option. Here's when to skip the DIY and dial:

  • You have no spare key and no one nearby with access.
  • You have kids, pets, or someone vulnerable inside.
  • You left the stove, iron, or another appliance on.
  • It's late, you're alone, or the weather is dangerous.
  • Your door has a deadbolt and you're not confident you can open it without damage.

A licensed mobile locksmith can open most residential locks in minutes without breaking anything. Professional locksmiths carry the tools and training to handle pin-tumbler deadbolts, knob locks, and even smart locks. You'll get back inside faster than waiting for a friend to drive across town, and your door stays intact.

Bonus: many locksmiths can rekey your locks on the spot so the old key no longer works—useful if you lost your keys somewhere public and you're worried about security.

What NOT to Do When Locked Out

Desperation leads to bad decisions. Avoid these common mistakes:

  • Do not try to pick the lock yourself. You'll probably jam the pins, scratch the cylinder, or break your makeshift tool off inside. Now a five-minute locksmith job becomes a lock replacement.
  • Do not kick the door. Exterior doors have solid frames and deadbolt strike plates. You're more likely to hurt your foot or hip than break the door. Even if you succeed, you'll split the jamb and face a $200+ repair.
  • Do not pry a window with a screwdriver or crowbar. Frames bend, glass breaks, and you've just created an entry point anyone can use later.
  • Do not climb onto your roof or a second-story ledge. Falls from height are a leading cause of home injury. Not worth it.
  • Do not call a random 1-800 locksmith from a Google ad. Some lead-gen companies dispatch unlicensed subcontractors who quote low then triple the price on-site. Use a local, licensed locksmith with a real phone number and verified reviews.

If your lockout involves a smart lock with a dead battery, some models have an external jump-port you can power with a 9V battery. Check your lock's manual online if you can access it from your phone. Otherwise, a locksmith can handle smart locks too—most have mechanical key overrides or can be bypassed safely with the right tools.

How to Prevent the Next Lockout

Once you're back inside, take fifteen minutes to lockout-proof your life:

  • Give a spare key to a trusted neighbor or family member within ten minutes of your home. Not a hidden rock. A real person.
  • Install a keyless deadbolt or smart lock with a backup code. Brands with ANSI/BHMA Grade 1 or Grade 2 ratings offer strong security and convenience. Many have smartphone apps so you can unlock remotely or generate temporary codes for guests.
  • Keep a spare car key in your wallet (not your keychain). If your house key is on the same ring as your car key and you lock both inside, you're stuck twice.
  • Use a lockbox. Realtors use them because they work. Mount one in a discreet spot—back gate, side fence—and memorize the code.
  • Check your deadbolt. If it's old, sticky, or the key doesn't turn smoothly, have a locksmith service it or rekey it. A failing lock is a lockout waiting to happen.

If you rent, ask your landlord or property manager if they keep a spare. Some do, some don't. Knowing ahead of time saves a panicked call later.

Can I open my locked door myself without a key?

It's possible but risky. Most DIY methods damage the lock, door, or frame. A professional locksmith has the tools to open your door in minutes without breaking anything, and can rekey it on the spot if needed.

How long does it take a locksmith to unlock a house door?

Typically 5–15 minutes once the locksmith arrives. Most pin-tumbler deadbolts and knob locks open quickly with professional tools. Smart locks and high-security cylinders may take a few minutes longer.

Will forcing my door open damage it?

Yes. Kicking or prying a door usually splits the jamb, bends the strike plate, or breaks the lock cylinder. Repair costs often exceed the price of a locksmith call, and you're left with a door that won't latch securely until fixed.
