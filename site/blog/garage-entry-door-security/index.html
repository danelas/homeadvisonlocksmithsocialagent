The Garage Entry Door: Your Home's Forgotten Weak Spot

Published June 30, 2026

Why Burglars Target the Garage Entry Door

Most forced entries don't start at your front door. They start in the garage. Once inside, the interior door connecting your garage to your home is often the weakest link — a basic privacy knob, a hollow-core slab, and sometimes no deadbolt at all.

Garage doors are compromised through poor habits: leaving the main door open during errands, using predictable keypad codes, or parking outside so the door stays unlocked. Once an intruder is inside the garage, that interior entry door is their ticket into your living space. If it's not secured, they're in.

Security experts call this the "two-door problem." Homeowners focus on perimeter locks — front, back, side entries — and overlook the garage-to-house connection. That door deserves the same attention as your front door.

What's Wrong With Most Garage Entry Doors

Walk to your garage right now and look at the door leading into your home. Chances are it has a standard passage or privacy lockset — the kind you'd find on a bedroom door. These knobs have no deadlocking latch and can be jimmied with a credit card in seconds.

Common problems:

  • No deadbolt. A twist-lock knob is not a security device.
  • Hollow-core door. Easy to kick through or punch a hole near the lock.
  • Short strike plate screws. Even if you have a deadbolt, half-inch screws into trim won't stop a shoulder.
  • No door viewer or window. You can't see who's in the garage before opening.

Fixing these issues doesn't require a full door replacement. Upgrading the lock hardware and reinforcing the frame can turn this door into a real barrier.

Upgrade to a Grade 1 Deadbolt and Reinforced Strike

Start with a single-cylinder deadbolt rated ANSI/BHMA Grade 1. That's the highest residential security standard — one million lock cycles, pick-resistant pins, hardened bolt. Brands like Schlage B60 or Kwikset 980 meet this spec and cost under $50.

Install a heavy-duty strike plate with three-inch screws that anchor into the wall stud, not just the door frame trim. A reinforced strike spreads kick force across a larger area and makes the door significantly harder to breach. For less than $20 in parts and fifteen minutes of labor, this upgrade is one of the best returns on investment in home security.

If the door is hollow-core, consider swapping it for a solid-core or steel door. Solid wood or steel composite doors are harder to kick through and provide better sound and fire resistance. Pair that with the upgraded deadbolt and strike, and you've created a real choke point.

When to Use a Double-Cylinder Deadbolt

If your garage entry door has a window or is within arm's reach of a garage window, a standard single-cylinder deadbolt (key outside, thumb-turn inside) becomes a liability. An intruder can break the glass and reach through to unlock it.

A double-cylinder deadbolt requires a key on both sides. No thumb-turn. That prevents the reach-through attack. However, it also creates a fire-exit concern — if the key isn't immediately accessible during an emergency, you could be trapped.

Code requirements vary by jurisdiction. Some municipalities prohibit double-cylinder locks on any egress door. If you go this route, keep a key on a nearby hook (out of arm's reach from the window) and make sure every household member knows where it is. A keypad deadbolt is another option — no glass-break vulnerability, and no key to misplace.

Add a Security Bar or Door Reinforcement Kit

If you want backup beyond the lock, a door security bar or floor brace adds a second layer. These devices wedge under the knob or bolt into the floor, making it nearly impossible to force the door inward even if the lock is compromised.

Another upgrade: a door reinforcement kit. These wrap metal plates around the lock and strike areas, distributing force across a larger surface. Kits like Door Armor or Defender Security run $30–$70 and install with basic tools. You're reinforcing the weakest structural points — the lock bore and the strike cutout.

Don't Forget the Garage Door Itself

Securing the interior entry door is critical, but it's not the only step. The main garage door should have a strong opener with rolling-code technology — fixed-code remotes are easy to clone. Always lock the manual release lever when you're away for extended periods; that T-handle is a known weak point.

If your garage has service doors or windows, secure them with keyed locks or pin bolts. A thief who can't get through the main door will try side entries. Motion-sensor lights and a visible security camera deter opportunistic crime.

Finally, never leave the garage door open unattended, even for "just a minute" while you unload groceries. That's the window of opportunity many burglars look for.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a deadbolt required on a garage entry door?

Building codes vary, but best practice is yes — treat it like any exterior door. A twist-lock knob alone offers minimal security. Install a Grade 1 deadbolt with a reinforced strike plate for real protection.

Can I use a smart lock on my garage-to-house door?

Absolutely. A smart deadbolt with remote locking, activity logs, and auto-lock features adds convenience and oversight. Just ensure it's rated for security (Grade 1 or 2) and that the door itself is solid-core or steel.

Should I use a single-cylinder or double-cylinder deadbolt?

Single-cylinder (key outside, thumb-turn inside) is standard. Use double-cylinder (key both sides) only if the door has glass within arm's reach — but check local fire code and keep an accessible key inside for emergencies.

What if my garage entry door is hollow-core?

A hollow-core door is easy to breach. Upgrade to solid-core wood or steel. Even with a great lock, a flimsy door won't stop a determined kick. Pair a strong door with a Grade 1 deadbolt and reinforced strike for full security.

How much does it cost to secure a garage entry door?

DIY hardware — a Grade 1 deadbolt, reinforced strike, and security bar — runs $50–$100. Professional installation and rekeying typically add $75–$150. A solid-core door replacement, if needed, is $150–$400 depending on size and material.
