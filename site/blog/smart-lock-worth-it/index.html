Are Smart Locks Worth It? An Honest Locksmith Take

Published June 21, 2026

What Smart Locks Actually Do (and Don't Do)

A smart lock replaces your deadbolt's interior thumbturn with electronics. You unlock the door via Bluetooth app, numeric keypad, fingerprint, or voice command through a smart-home hub. Most models keep a physical keyway on the outside as a backup, though some are entirely keyless.

What they don't do: replace your door, fix a warped frame, or strengthen a hollow-core entry. Security starts with a solid door, reinforced strike plate, and proper installation. A smart lock on a weak door is like a high-tech padlock on cardboard.

Most smart locks fit standard deadbolt backsets (2⅜″ or 2¾″) and door-prep measurements (1½″ to 1¾″ thick, 2⅛″ bore). If your door was prepped for a standard ANSI-grade deadbolt, retrofit is usually straightforward. Older or non-standard doors may need professional help—our mobile locksmiths handle smart-lock installs and compatibility checks daily.

Real Pros: Where Smart Locks Shine

No more spare-key hiding. Cleaners, dog-walkers, and guests get temporary codes you can delete remotely. No rocks, fake sprinklers, or under-mat keys for burglars to find.

Remote locking. Forgot to lock up? Check and lock from your phone if the lock has Wi-Fi or a hub connection. Bluetooth-only models require you to be within range (typically 30–50 feet).

Activity logs. See who unlocked the door and when. Useful for rental properties, Airbnb, or keeping tabs on teens.

Integration. Pair with Ring, Google Home, Alexa, or Apple HomeKit. "Alexa, lock the front door" works if your ecosystem supports it.

These features deliver real convenience if you already live on your phone and trust app-based security. If you never carry your phone around the house or prefer tactile keys, the value drops fast.

Real Cons: The Trade-Offs You'll Live With

Batteries die. Most smart locks run on four AA batteries, lasting 6–12 months depending on usage. Low-battery warnings appear in the app, but if you ignore them, you're locked out. Keep spare batteries on hand and a physical key accessible.

Connectivity hiccups. Bluetooth can drop if your phone's in another room. Wi-Fi locks depend on your home network—router down means no remote access. Z-Wave and Zigbee models require a compatible hub.

Price. Entry-level smart locks start around $150; name-brand models with full integration run $200–$350. A quality mechanical deadbolt costs $40–$80. You're paying for electronics and app development, not necessarily stronger physical security.

App dependence. If the manufacturer discontinues support or shuts down servers, features can vanish. Always choose locks with physical backup—keypad or keyway—so you're never stranded by a software sunset.

We've seen homeowners frustrated by firmware bugs, connectivity drops during storms, and lockouts when phone batteries die. These aren't dealbreakers, but they're real.

Security: Are They Safer Than Traditional Deadbolts?

Smart locks and mechanical deadbolts share the same ANSI/BHMA grading system—Grade 1 (best), Grade 2 (residential standard), Grade 3 (light-duty). A Grade 1 smart lock is mechanically as strong as a Grade 1 traditional deadbolt. The electronics add different vulnerabilities, not necessarily weaker ones.

Physical attacks: A smart lock's bolt and strike resist picking, bumping, and drilling the same way a mechanical lock does—assuming equal build quality. Cheap smart locks skimp on the cylinder; look for models with bump-resistant pins or sidebar mechanisms.

Electronic attacks: Bluetooth and Wi-Fi can theoretically be hacked, but exploits require proximity and skill. Most break-ins are smash-and-grab, not Ocean's Eleven. A burglar will kick the door or break a window before trying to brute-force your lock's encryption.

Bottom line: A quality smart lock (August, Yale, Schlage Encode, Kwikset Halo) installed on a solid door with a reinforced strike plate is at least as secure as a good mechanical deadbolt. It's not weaker—just a different risk profile.

Who Should Buy a Smart Lock (and Who Shouldn't)

Good fit if you:

  • Manage rental properties or Airbnb and need to issue/revoke codes remotely
  • Have kids who lose keys regularly
  • Already use a smart-home ecosystem and want one less thing to remember
  • Value activity logs for peace of mind
  • Are comfortable troubleshooting app issues and swapping batteries

Skip it if you:

  • Prefer zero dependence on apps, Wi-Fi, or batteries
  • Live in an area with spotty internet or frequent power outages
  • Are on a tight budget—a mechanical Grade 1 deadbolt offers excellent security for a fraction of the cost
  • Rarely use your smartphone or find tech interfaces frustrating

Smart locks are tools, not magic. They solve specific problems—keyless entry, remote access, temporary codes—but introduce new dependencies. If those problems aren't yours, a traditional deadbolt rekeyed to your spec is simpler and cheaper.

Installation and Compatibility: What to Watch For

Most smart locks are marketed as DIY-friendly. If you're handy and your door already has a standard deadbolt, you can likely swap it in 20–30 minutes with a screwdriver. Check three things first:

  • Backset: Distance from door edge to center of the bore hole (2⅜″ or 2¾″ standard)
  • Door thickness: 1⅜″ to 1¾″ is typical; thicker or thinner doors may need adapters
  • Cross-bore diameter: Most locks expect a 2⅛″ hole; if yours is different, you may need a new drill or professional help

Non-standard doors—metal commercial doors, old wood with unusual prep, or doors with existing smart locks from different brands—can be tricky. The Verdict: Worth It for the Right Homeowner

Smart locks aren't a universal upgrade. They're worth it if keyless convenience, remote access, or temporary codes solve a real problem in your life. They're not worth it if you value simplicity, hate recharging/replacing batteries, or don't trust app-based systems.

From a locksmith perspective: a well-chosen smart lock on a properly reinforced door is secure. But so is a Grade 1 mechanical deadbolt installed correctly. Security comes from the whole package—solid door, long screws in the strike plate, good lighting, and neighbors who notice strangers.

If you decide to go smart, buy a reputable brand with a physical backup (key or keypad), check reviews for firmware stability, and budget for batteries. If you skip it, invest the savings in a high-security mechanical deadbolt, a wide-angle peephole, and maybe a video doorbell. Both paths work; pick the one that fits how you actually live.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can smart locks be hacked?

Theoretically yes, but real-world exploits are rare and require proximity and skill. Most burglars use physical force—kicking doors or breaking windows—rather than hacking Bluetooth or Wi-Fi. A quality smart lock from a reputable brand is no less secure than a traditional deadbolt.

What happens if the battery dies in my smart lock?

You'll see low-battery warnings in the app weeks before it dies. If it does die, use your physical backup key (if your model has one) or a 9V battery held to external terminals on some models to power it temporarily. Always keep spare AA batteries and a backup key accessible.

Do I need Wi-Fi for a smart lock?

Not always. Bluetooth-only locks work via your phone when you're nearby (30–50 feet). Wi-Fi or hub-based locks (Z-Wave, Zigbee) enable remote access and integration with smart-home systems, but they depend on your home network.

Can I install a smart lock myself?

If your door has a standard deadbolt already and you're comfortable with a screwdriver, most smart locks install in 20–30 minutes. Non-standard doors, unusual backsets, or alignment issues may need a locksmith to avoid motor strain and battery drain.

Are smart locks as strong as regular deadbolts?

Yes, if they carry the same ANSI/BHMA grade. A Grade 1 smart lock is mechanically as strong as a Grade 1 mechanical deadbolt. The bolt, strike, and cylinder quality matter more than whether it's electronic or traditional.
