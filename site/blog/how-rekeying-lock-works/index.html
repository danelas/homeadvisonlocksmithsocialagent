How Rekeying a Lock Actually Works

Published June 13, 2026

What Is Rekeying?

Rekeying means changing the internal pins inside a lock cylinder so a new key operates it. The old key no longer works. The lock hardware — the exterior knob, deadbolt body, strike plate — stays in place. You're swapping out tiny components inside the plug, not the entire lock.

Most pin-tumbler locks can be rekeyed in minutes. Wafer locks (common in filing cabinets and some older car doors) use flat wafers instead of pins, but the principle is the same: rearrange the internal tumblers to match a new key cut.

Rekeying is not the same as lock replacement. If your deadbolt is damaged, rusted, or low-grade, replacement makes more sense. If the lock body is solid but you need a new key — after a break-in, lost keys, or a tenant move-out — rekeying is usually the right call.

Inside the Cylinder: How Pin-Tumbler Locks Work

A standard pin-tumbler deadbolt has a rotating plug inside a fixed cylinder. When you insert the correct key, it lifts a series of spring-loaded pins to a precise height. Each pin is actually two pieces: a bottom pin (touches the key) and a top pin (sits above it). When all the splits between bottom and top pins align at the shear line, the plug can rotate and the lock opens.

Insert the wrong key, and at least one pin pair sits too high or too low. The top pin jams into the plug, blocking rotation. That's the lock's entire security mechanism — alignment at the shear line.

Most residential deadbolts use five or six pins. High-security locks add side pins, mushroom pins, or spool pins to resist picking and bumping, but the core idea stays the same.

The Rekeying Process Step-by-Step

A locksmith removes the lock cylinder from the door. For a deadbolt, that usually means unscrewing the interior thumb-turn assembly. For a knob or lever, the cylinder might pull out with a special tool or after removing a retaining clip.

With the cylinder on a workbench, the locksmith inserts the current working key and rotates the plug. That aligns the pins at the shear line so the plug can slide out of the housing. Once the plug is out, the old pins drop into a tray.

The locksmith selects new bottom pins sized to match the cuts on your new key. Each cut depth corresponds to a numbered pin height. The springs and top pins usually stay the same unless they're worn. The new bottom pins go into the plug chambers, the plug slides back into the cylinder, and the locksmith tests the new key. If it turns smoothly, rekeying is done. The cylinder goes back into the door.

Total time for one lock: five to ten minutes if there are no complications. Rekeying multiple locks to the same key — so one key opens your front door, back door, and garage entry — takes a bit longer but costs less than buying all new locksets.

When to Rekey vs. Replace

Rekey when:

  • You lost keys or gave out copies you want to invalidate
  • You bought a home and don't know who has keys from previous owners
  • A roommate or tenant moved out
  • The lock cylinder works fine but you want all locks on one key
  • You want to upgrade to a restricted keyway (requires special blanks)

Replace when:

  • The lock is damaged, sticky, or won't latch properly
  • You're upgrading from a builder-grade lock (often ANSI/BHMA Grade 3) to a Grade 1 deadbolt
  • The lock is outdated and you want smart-lock features or keyless entry
  • The backset or bore size doesn't match your door prep (common in older homes)
  • You had a break-in and the cylinder was forced or drilled

Master Keying and Keying Alike

Rekeying also enables master-key systems. A master key opens multiple locks, while each lock also has its own unique change key. Landlords use this so tenants have keys to their units, but the landlord's master opens every door.

Technically, you add extra shear lines inside the cylinder — more than one correct pin height per chamber. It's more complex than standard rekeying and slightly reduces pick resistance, but it's convenient for property managers and small business owners.

Keying alike is simpler: rekeying several locks to operate on the same single key. No master hierarchy, just one key for your front door, back door, deadbolt, and garage. Most locksmiths can key alike any locks with compatible keyways — Kwikset to Kwikset, Schlage to Schlage. Mixing brands usually requires replacing cylinders or using adaptor kits.

Can You Rekey a Lock Yourself?

Rekey kits exist for popular brands like Kwikset and Schlage. They include new pins, a follower tool, and instructions. If you're comfortable with small parts and have a steady hand, it's possible. You'll need the current working key, the new key you want to use, and about thirty minutes per lock while you learn the process.

Mistakes are easy. Drop a spring and you'll spend twenty minutes searching the floor. Mix up pin sizes and the lock won't turn. Install the plug backward and the keyway faces the wrong direction. None of these ruin the lock permanently, but they're frustrating.

For one or two locks and no time pressure, DIY can work. For an entire house, after a break-in, or if you need it done today, calling a mobile locksmith is faster and safer. Rekeying and Security: What It Does and Doesn't Fix

Rekeying instantly invalidates old keys. If you're worried someone has a copy, rekeying solves that. But it doesn't fix a weak lock. A builder-grade deadbolt with a short throw and a flimsy strike plate is still vulnerable after rekeying — you've just changed which key opens it.

Pair rekeying with upgrades: a longer deadbolt throw (one inch is standard, but some go to 1.5 inches), a reinforced strike plate with three-inch screws into the door frame, and a Grade 1 cylinder with anti-pick pins. That's a real security improvement.

Rekeying also won't help if someone can bypass the lock entirely — sliding a credit card past a spring-latch, prying a door with a weak frame, or entering through an unlocked window. Think of rekeying as one piece of a larger security picture. It's important, but not a magic solution.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does rekeying a lock cost?

Rekeying a single lock typically costs $20–$30 in labor, plus a service call fee if it's the only job. Rekeying multiple locks at once is cheaper per lock because the locksmith is already on-site. Expect $75–$150 for a full house rekey, depending on how many locks you have.

Can any lock be rekeyed?

Most pin-tumbler and wafer locks can be rekeyed. Some high-security and electronic locks have proprietary cylinders that require special pins or aren't designed for rekeying. A locksmith can tell you on inspection whether your specific lock is rekeyable.

How long does rekeying take?

A professional locksmith can rekey one lock in five to ten minutes. Rekeying a whole house — four to six locks — usually takes thirty to sixty minutes, including setup and testing each lock with the new key.

Will rekeying damage my lock?

No. Rekeying replaces internal pins but doesn't alter the lock body, cylinder housing, or door hardware. If done correctly, the lock works exactly as it did before, just with a different key.

Can I rekey locks to match a key I already have?

Yes. If you want new locks to work with an existing key, a locksmith can cut the pins to match that key's bitting. This is common when adding a new deadbolt and you want it on the same key as your front door.
