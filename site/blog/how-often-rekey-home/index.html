How Often Should You Rekey Your Home?

Published June 25, 2026

The Short Answer: Event-Based, Not Calendar-Based

There's no magic annual schedule. Most homes need rekeying when something changes — a move, a breakup, a lost key. If none of those happen, a rekey every 5-7 years covers gradual pin wear and the slow accumulation of key copies you've lost track of.

Pin-tumbler deadbolts use small brass pins that can wear down over thousands of key insertions. Worn pins make bumping attacks slightly easier. More common: you've handed out keys to contractors, dog walkers, or ex-tenants, and you're not sure who still has a copy.

Think of rekeying as inexpensive insurance. A locksmith swaps the pins inside your existing lock in minutes. You keep the same hardware; only the key changes. Cost is far lower than replacing every lock.

Rekey Immediately After These Events

Moving into a new home or apartment. You have no idea how many keys the previous owner gave to neighbors, cleaners, or relatives. Even if you trust the seller, rekey before you unpack. This is the single most important trigger.

Lost or stolen keys. If your keys vanish and you can't confirm they're destroyed, rekey that day. A key with no address attached is low-risk, but if your wallet or purse was stolen with ID, someone knows where you live.

Relationship or roommate changes. Breakups, divorces, and roommate departures all mean someone who once had access no longer should. Asking for keys back isn't enough — copies are cheap and fast to make.

After a contractor or service worker. If you gave a key to a plumber, painter, or house sitter and the job is done, rekey or collect every copy. Better yet, use a temporary code on a smart lock so you never hand out a physical key.

Signs You've Waited Too Long

Keys stick or feel rough. Worn pins or a dirty keyway can make your key hard to turn. Sometimes cleaning and lubrication fix it, but if the lock is old, worn pins mean it's time to rekey or upgrade to a higher-grade deadbolt.

You can't remember who has keys. If you've lived in the same place for years and handed out spares to multiple people, you've lost control of access. A rekey resets that list to zero.

Your lock is builder-grade. Many new homes come with ANSI Grade 3 locks — functional but minimal security. If you've never upgraded, combine a rekey with a move to Grade 1 or Grade 2 hardware. A mobile locksmith can handle both in one visit.

You've had a break-in attempt. Even if the door held, forced entry can damage the lock cylinder or pins. Rekey and inspect the strike plate, deadbolt throw, and door frame for damage.

Rekey vs. Replace: Which Do You Need?

Rekeying swaps the pins inside your existing lock so old keys no longer work. It's fast and cheap — usually under $30 per lock if you already have the locksmith on-site for another job.

Replace the lock if the hardware is damaged, outdated, or low-grade. If you're upgrading from a passage knob to a keyed deadbolt, or from Grade 3 to Grade 1, replacement is required. If you want to match a new key to locks across your house, a locksmith can rekey everything to one key (called keying alike).

Most move-in situations only need a rekey. If the deadbolt is solid and the door frame is reinforced, new pins are all you need. A locksmith can assess on-site and quote both options before starting work.

Smart Locks and Electronic Access

Smart locks eliminate most rekeying scenarios. You issue temporary codes to guests or contractors, then delete them when access should end. No physical key changes hands.

But smart locks still have a mechanical backup keyway. If someone has that backup key, they bypass all your app controls. When you move in or lose the backup, rekey the mechanical cylinder even if you rely on codes day-to-day.

Some smart locks use proprietary keyways that only the manufacturer can rekey. Check before you buy. Standard keyways (Kwikset, Schlage) give you more locksmith options and lower long-term costs.

The 5-7 Year Maintenance Rekey

If you've had no key loss, no move, and no access changes, a rekey every 5-7 years is smart preventive maintenance. Pins wear. Keys get copied and forgotten. Locks collect grime that makes them easier to manipulate.

Combine this maintenance rekey with other upgrades: reinforce your strike plate with 3-inch screws into the door frame, check that your deadbolt throw extends at least one inch, and confirm all exterior doors have ANSI Grade 1 or Grade 2 deadbolts.

A mobile locksmith can handle all of this in one visit. You'll have fresh pins, verified hardware, and a clear record of who has keys. For most homeowners, that peace of mind is worth the modest cost every few years.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to rekey a house?

Rekeying a single lock typically costs $20-$50 depending on the lock type and your location. Many locksmiths offer discounts when rekeying multiple locks in one visit. Rekeying is much cheaper than replacing hardware.

Can I rekey my own locks?

Yes, if you buy a rekeying kit for your lock brand and have some mechanical skill. But most homeowners find a locksmith faster and more reliable — pins are tiny, easy to drop, and the wrong pin height leaves your lock insecure.

Should I rekey or change locks when I move in?

Rekey unless the hardware is damaged or low-grade. Rekeying is faster and cheaper, and you keep the existing finish and style. If the lock is builder-grade or worn, upgrade to a Grade 1 or Grade 2 deadbolt at the same time.

How long does rekeying a lock take?

A skilled locksmith can rekey a standard pin-tumbler deadbolt in 10-15 minutes. Rekeying a whole house of five or six locks usually takes under an hour, including setup and cleanup.

Do I need to rekey if I have a smart lock?

Yes, if someone has the mechanical backup key. Smart locks let you delete access codes, but the physical keyway is a permanent backdoor unless you rekey it. Always rekey the backup cylinder when you move in or lose a key.
