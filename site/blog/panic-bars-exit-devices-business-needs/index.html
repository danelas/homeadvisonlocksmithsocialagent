Panic Bars & Exit Devices: What Your Business Needs

Published June 20, 2026

What Is a Panic Bar (Exit Device)?

A panic bar is a horizontal push-bar mounted on the interior of a commercial door. Press it, and the latch retracts—no twisting a knob, no key. The official name is exit device, and it's mandated by fire and building codes in assembly, educational, and high-occupancy spaces.

Exit devices fall into three types: rim (surface latch on the door frame), mortise (lockset integrated into the door edge), and vertical rod (rods engage top and bottom of the frame). Each suits different door configurations—hollow-metal, aluminum storefront, wood—and different security or weather requirements.

If you're retrofitting or building new, knowing which device fits your opening saves rework and meets inspector sign-off the first time.

ANSI/BHMA Grades: What 1, 2, and 3 Mean

Exit devices carry ANSI/BHMA grades just like locksets. Grade 1 is the highest—tested for 500,000 cycles, designed for high-traffic entries like schools, hospitals, or retail. Grade 2 handles moderate use (office suites, smaller warehouses). Grade 3 is light-duty and rarely seen on commercial egress doors.

Your architect or code official will specify the grade. If you're replacing old hardware, match or exceed the original grade. Installing a Grade 3 device on a door that sees hundreds of exits a day invites mechanical failure and liability.

Code Requirements: When You Must Install Exit Devices

The International Building Code (IBC) and NFPA 101 Life Safety Code require exit devices on doors serving spaces with occupant loads typically above 50, and in educational occupancies regardless of count. Local amendments vary—some jurisdictions drop the threshold to 30 or add requirements for assembly, day-care, or high-hazard uses.

Fire marshal inspections check that exit devices are UL-listed for panic hardware, latch properly when the door closes, and don't require more than 15 pounds of force to operate. Chains, padlocks, or any fastener that prevents free egress will fail inspection and trigger fines.

If you're unsure whether your building needs panic bars, pull your certificate of occupancy or call your local building department. Installing compliant hardware before an audit is cheaper than retrofitting under a violation notice.

Fire-Rated Doors and Exit-Device Compatibility

Many exit doors are also fire-rated—20-minute, 60-minute, 90-minute labels are common. The exit device must carry a matching fire rating and be installed exactly per the manufacturer's template. Drill an extra hole or mount the strike plate wrong, and you void the fire rating.

Fire-rated assemblies also restrict dogging—the feature that lets you prop the push-bar in the retracted position. Code permits dogging on non-fire-rated doors in some occupancies, but never on fire egress. If an inspector finds a dogged fire door, expect a stop-work or occupancy-hold.

Trim Options: Pull Handles, Key Cylinders, and Access Control

The exterior side of a panic-bar door needs trim—a pull handle, lever, or dummy knob. You can add a key cylinder for after-hours entry, or integrate electric strikes and card readers for access control. Many modern systems unlock the strike on credential swipe but leave free egress always active from inside.

Avoid double-cylinder deadbolts on egress doors. Requiring a key to exit is a code violation in commercial occupancies. Panic hardware exists to let people out instantly, even in the dark or under smoke.

If you're upgrading to electronic access, your locksmith coordinates with your low-voltage contractor—power-transfer hinges, request-to-exit sensors, and mag-lock fail-safe wiring all tie together. One mis-wired component and the door won't latch or won't release. Our commercial services include full access-control integration and testing.

Maintenance and Inspection: Keeping Exit Devices Compliant

Exit devices need periodic checks. Latch bolts wear, push-pads loosen, and vertical rods slip out of adjustment. Monthly self-inspections—push the bar, confirm the door latches when released, verify no excessive play—catch most issues early.

Annual professional service includes lubricating pivot points, checking strike alignment, testing dogging mechanisms (where allowed), and confirming UL labels are intact. If your device shows rust, bent rods, or a spongy push-bar, schedule repair before it fails mid-evacuation.

Insurance and liability hinge on working egress. A jammed panic bar during a fire isn't just a code problem—it's a catastrophic one. Budget for maintenance the same way you budget for fire-extinguisher inspections.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I install a panic bar on any door?

Panic bars fit most commercial doors—wood, hollow-metal, aluminum—but the door must be thick enough (usually 1¾ inches minimum) and properly prepped. Fire-rated doors require listed hardware and exact template drilling.

What's the difference between panic hardware and fire-exit hardware?

Panic hardware meets egress-speed requirements. Fire-exit hardware adds a fire rating (20, 60, or 90 minutes) and restricts dogging. Both allow free egress, but fire-exit hardware must stay latched to contain smoke and flame.

Do I need a key cylinder on the outside of a panic-bar door?

Not required by code, but common for after-hours entry. You can also use card readers or keypads. Never install a double-cylinder deadbolt that requires a key to exit from inside.

How often should exit devices be inspected?

Monthly self-checks (push the bar, confirm latching) and annual professional service. High-traffic or exterior devices may need service every six months to address weather wear and heavy use.

Can I add an alarm to a panic-bar door?

Yes. Alarmed exit devices sound when the bar is pushed, deterring unauthorized exit while still allowing free egress. They're popular in retail, schools, and hospitals to prevent theft or wandering.
