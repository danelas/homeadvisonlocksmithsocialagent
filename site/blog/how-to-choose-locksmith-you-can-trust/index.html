How to Choose a Locksmith You Can Trust

Published June 26, 2026

Why Choosing the Right Locksmith Matters

You're locked out. You Google "locksmith near me." Three ads pop up with 800 numbers and $15 promises. You call. An hour later, someone shows up in an unmarked van, drills your lock, and hands you a $400 bill.

This happens every day. Scam locksmiths use fake listings, bait pricing, and high-pressure tactics. They damage locks that could have been opened safely. They quote one price on the phone and triple it on-site.

A legitimate locksmith is transparent. They tell you who they are, what the job will cost, and show proof of licensing and insurance. Knowing what to look for protects your wallet and your property.

Red Flags: How to Spot a Locksmith Scam

Start with the phone call. If the person answering can't give you a business name or physical address, hang up. Scammers use generic names like "Locksmith Services" and route calls through national call centers.

Watch for vague pricing. A real locksmith asks questions—what type of lock, what's the problem, are you locked out or replacing hardware—and gives you a quote range. If they say "starting at $15" or refuse to estimate, that's a trap.

Check the vehicle and uniform. Legitimate mobile locksmiths arrive in marked vans with company names and phone numbers. They wear uniforms or ID badges. If someone pulls up in a personal car with no identification, don't let them start work.

Drilling should be a last resort. Most residential lockouts—standard pin-tumbler deadbolts and knob locks—can be picked or bumped without damage. If the first thing they reach for is a drill, question it. Destroying a lock is faster and lets them charge more for replacement.

Verify Licensing and Insurance

Many states and counties require locksmiths to hold licenses. Ask for the license number before booking. Look it up on your state's licensing board website. An unlicensed locksmith has no accountability and no recourse if something goes wrong.

Insurance matters just as much. A bonded and insured locksmith covers damage to your door, frame, or lock if they make a mistake. Ask for proof of general liability and bonding. If they can't provide it, move on.

Demand Transparent, Up-Front Pricing

A trustworthy locksmith quotes the full price before touching your lock. That quote should include labor, parts, service call fees, and any after-hours surcharges. No surprises.

Ask these questions on the phone: What's the service call fee? What's the labor rate? How much for the lock or key if replacement is needed? Do you charge extra for nights, weekends, or emergencies?

Scammers lowball to get in the door, then claim the job is "more complicated" once they arrive. They add fees for mileage, "difficulty," or invented problems. Refuse to pay inflated bills. In many jurisdictions, you can dispute charges if the final cost is dramatically higher than the phone quote.

Check Reviews and Ask for References

Look beyond star ratings. Read the reviews. Do multiple customers mention the same technician by name? Do the reviews describe specific services—rekeying, car lockouts, deadbolt installation? Generic five-star reviews ("Great service, very professional!") can be faked.

Check Google, Yelp, and the Better Business Bureau. Look for patterns. If half the reviews praise speed and the other half complain about price gouging, that's a problem. A few negative reviews are normal—no business is perfect—but consistent complaints about billing or damage are dealbreakers.

Don't be afraid to ask for references, especially for commercial work or master key systems. A locksmith who's installed ANSI Grade 1 deadbolts or set up sub-master keying for a property manager should be able to point you to past clients.

Response Time and Availability

Emergencies don't wait for business hours. A locksmith who only works 9 to 5 isn't much help when you're locked out at midnight. Look for 24/7 availability.

Ask for an estimated arrival time. Most mobile locksmiths can reach you within 30 to 60 minutes in urban and suburban areas, longer in rural zones. Be wary of anyone who promises "15 minutes guaranteed"—that's usually a call center dispatching the closest contractor, who may or may not be qualified.

What to Do If You've Been Scammed

If a locksmith overcharges, damages your property, or refuses to leave until you pay, stay calm. Do not pay cash. Use a credit card if possible—you can dispute the charge later.

Document everything. Take photos of the technician, the vehicle, the work done, and any damage. Get a written invoice with the business name, address, and technician's name. File a complaint with your state's consumer protection agency and the Better Business Bureau.

In extreme cases—if the locksmith threatens you or refuses to return your keys—call the police. Extortion and fraud are criminal offenses.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I verify a locksmith is legitimate before hiring them?

Ask for their business name, physical address, and license number. Look up the license on your state's licensing board website. Check online reviews on Google and the Better Business Bureau. A legitimate locksmith will have verifiable credentials and a real storefront or service area.

What should a locksmith quote include?

A complete quote includes the service call fee, labor rate, cost of any parts or locks, and surcharges for after-hours or emergency service. The locksmith should provide this before starting work, and the final bill should match unless the scope changes with your approval.

Is it normal for a locksmith to drill my lock?

Drilling should be a last resort. Most residential locks—pin-tumbler deadbolts and standard knob locks—can be picked or manipulated without damage. If a locksmith reaches for a drill immediately, ask why and consider getting a second opinion.

Can I dispute a locksmith bill if it's higher than the phone quote?

Yes. If the final bill is significantly higher than the quoted price and the locksmith didn't explain or get approval for the change, you can dispute the charge with your credit card company or file a complaint with your state's consumer protection agency.

Do locksmiths need to be licensed and insured?

In most states and counties, yes. Licensing requirements vary, but legitimate locksmiths carry liability insurance and bonding to cover accidental damage. Always ask for proof of licensing and insurance before hiring.
